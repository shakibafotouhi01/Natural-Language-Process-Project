THE GOLDEN GOOSE

In a fertile land of ancient Persia, there lived a humble man with three sons. The youngest, called Rustam the Simple, was ridiculed and scorned by all, a gentle soul seen as foolish.

One day, the eldest son planned to enter the vast Zagros forest to gather fragrant cedar wood. Before he set out, his mother gave him a sweet honey cake laced with rosewater and a flask of pomegranate wine, to stave off hunger and thirst.

As he journeyed beneath the ancient cypress trees, he encountered a small, wizened dervish with a snowy beard. The dervish greeted him warmly and pleaded, “Share but a morsel of your cake and a sip of your wine; my hunger and thirst weigh heavy.” But the eldest son, proud and shrewd, replied, “If I share my cake and wine, I shall have none for myself. Begone!” and he strode on, leaving the poor man behind.

When he tried to chop a towering cedar, his axe slipped, and he wounded his arm deeply. He had no choice but to return home, his pride wounded as well. This misfortune was the dervish’s subtle retribution.

The second son next ventured out, his mother again sending him off with a fragrant saffron cake and a jar of mulberry wine. The same dervish appeared and asked for a share, but the second son cunningly said, “If I give away my food and drink, I’ll be left empty. Farewell!” Ignoring the plea, he went on, only to soon strike his own leg with the axe, forcing him home in pain.

Finally, Rustam the Simple begged earnestly, “Father, let me go and gather wood too.” The father hesitated, “Your brothers were harmed; such work is not for you.” But Rustam’s pleas would not cease, and at last his father relented, “Go then, and learn by your own trials.”

His mother, with less hope, gave him a humble cake baked in ashes and a bottle of vinegary sour drink from fermented barley.

In the forest, the old dervish found Rustam, greeting him with a humble smile and repeating his request for cake and wine. Rustam replied kindly, “All I have is this ash-baked cake and sour beer; if you are content with them, let us share.” They sat beneath a fragrant jasmine tree, and as Rustam offered his simple fare, it transformed— the ash cake becoming a sweetened delicacy rich with honey and almonds, the sour beer turning to fine Shiraz wine. They feasted together, and the dervish said, “Because your heart is generous, I bless you with fortune. There stands an ancient cypress—fell it, and you will find a treasure at its roots.” Then he vanished as mysteriously as he came.

Rustam cut down the tree, and beneath it nestled a magnificent goose with feathers of gleaming gold, shimmering like the sun over the desert dunes. He lifted the golden bird and journeyed to an inn to rest.

The innkeeper’s three daughters, curious about the wondrous bird, longed to possess a golden feather. The eldest crept close and grasped a feather, but instantly her hand clung fast to the goose’s wing. Her sister soon followed, grasping her sibling, and found herself stuck as well. The youngest, undeterred by their cries, approached and touched her sisters, becoming bound to them. So, they were forced to spend the night tethered to Rustam’s golden goose.

The next dawn, Rustam set off, ignoring the weighty procession trailing behind him. The three maidens clung fast as he strode through the winding valleys and sun-dappled plains.

Along their path, a mullah encountered the curious train. “What folly is this—young women chasing a man through the fields?” he scolded and seized the youngest daughter’s hand, but he too became bound and was pulled along.

Soon after, the mullah’s assistant came across the scene and was drawn into the odd procession. Then two farmers, working their fields with curved sickles, were compelled to join, and now seven figures followed Rustam, laughing at their own predicament.

At last, they arrived at the court of a powerful Shah whose daughter was veiled not only in splendor but sorrow— she never laughed, and no joy could break her stern exterior. The Shah declared that whoever could make his daughter laugh would win her hand.

When the princess glimpsed the curious sight of the chained group behind Rustam, she burst into unexpected laughter, the sound like silver bells across the palace gardens. Rustam asked for her hand, but the Shah, displeased with the simple youth, demanded impossible trials first.

His first task was to find a man who could drink an entire cellar of wine. Rustam returned to the forest where the dervish had sat, now a sorrowful figure thirsting beyond all relief, having emptied vast barrels yet still parched.

“Come with me,” said Rustam, “and drink to your heart’s content.”

At the Shah’s cellar, the man drank deeply until every barrel was empty, his thirst finally quenched.

The Shah then declared Rustam must find a man who could consume a mountain of bread. Rustam ventured again and found a gaunt giant tying his belt tighter about his waist, declaring his hunger was endless despite feasting on a full oven of baked flatbreads.

“Come, you shall fill your hunger,” said Rustam.

At the palace an enormous mound of freshly baked bread was presented, and the giant devoured it all by day’s end.

Now the Shah demanded that Rustam produce a ship that could travel both on land and on water.

Guided once more to the forest, Rustam found the dervish who rewarded his kindness by gifting him a wondrous vessel, shaped like a swift gazelle and enchanted to glide across both earth and river.

Seeing this marvel, the Shah could no longer deny Rustam his daughter’s hand. A grand wedding was celebrated, and after the Shah’s passing, Rustam inherited the kingdom, living many prosperous years with his joyful bride. Thus, the humble and kind-hearted simpleton was honored, his golden goose a symbol of fortune born from generosity and grace.
