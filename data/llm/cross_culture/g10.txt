THE RAVEN

Once, in a flourishing realm of ancient Persia, there lived a queen blessed with a little daughter, a child still too tender to wander the palace gardens alone. One day, the child grew restless and tearful, and no matter how gently the queen tried to soothe her, the cries would not cease. Overcome with weariness, the queen gazed out the lattice window toward the dark cypress trees, where flocks of black ravens wheeled in the gilded sunlight. In a moment of despair, she whispered, “Oh, that my daughter might become a raven and fly away—then I would find but a moment’s peace.”

No sooner had the words left her lips than her beloved child transformed, feathers sprouting swiftly where soft skin had been. The little raven flapped its wings and darted through the open window, vanishing into the shadowed groves beyond the palace walls. The queen and king searched far and wide but heard no word of their lost princess.

Some time later, a determined traveler wandered through the same forest when his ears caught the familiar cawing of a raven. Drawn to the voice, he found the bird perched upon a low branch. “I was born a king’s daughter,” the raven spoke softly, “but a sorcerer’s spell holds me captive in this form. You alone can break my bondage.”

“What must I do?” asked the traveler.

“Venture deeper into the woods until you find a modest dwelling, where an old woman dwells. She will offer you food and wine, but refuse her gifts, lest you fall into a cursed slumber and fail to free me. In the garden behind her house is a great pile of fragrant cedar leaves; on that you must stand watch. For three days, each at the stroke of afternoon, I shall come in my royal carriage. The first day, drawn by four gleaming white horses; the second, by four fiery chestnuts; the third, by four coal-black steeds. If you sleep while I arrive, my freedom will never be yours to claim.”

The traveler swore he would heed her warning, but the raven’s eyes clouded with doubt. “Alas, I fear you will falter, despite your promises.”

When he reached the dwelling and entered, the old woman welcomed him warmly. “Friend, you look weary. Rest and partake of bread and sweet wine,” she urged.

“I cannot eat or drink,” he said firmly.

She pressed on until, with weakening will, he sipped the wine.

As the first appointed hour drew near, the traveler climbed upon the cedar heap, eyes fixed on the horizon. Fatigue enveloped him like a silk garment, and though he fought it fiercely, sleep claimed him. When the princess came riding by her four white horses, she sighed, “He has fallen,” and found him slumbering deeply despite her calls.

The next day, the same scene repeated. The traveler resisted at first but finally drank again from the old woman’s cup. Exhausted, he succumbed to sleep as the princess approached in her carriage drawn by chestnut steeds. She found him once more unawakened and sorrowfully left him.

On the third day, the old woman pressed the traveler with even greater insistence. Scenting the sweet wine, he drank despite himself. That afternoon, heavier than before, he fell into a deep sleep. The princess arrived behind her black horses, more mournful than ever. Unable to rouse him, she placed beside him an enchanted loaf of bread, smoked meats, and a flask of wine—gifts that would never diminish regardless of how much he partook. From her finger she removed a ring inscribed with her name and slipped it onto his hand. Finally, she left a letter:

“As long as you remain here, you cannot release me. Yet if your heart remains steadfast, seek the Golden Fortress of Shahrazad. There lies the power you need.”

She returned to her carriage and vanished toward the distant Golden Fortress.

When the traveler awoke, sorrow washed over him for he feared all was lost. Yet reading the letter filled him with resolve. He set out to find this fortress, journeying through shadowed forests for many days, until twilight found him too weary to go further. Under a tamarisk tree, he slept.

On the next dusk, strange cries stirred him—the howling of jackals and the groans of unseen spirits. Guided by a flickering light, he came upon a humble hut overshadowed by a giant sentinel. The towering giant declared, “You are a fortunate guest—for I have naught to eat and hunger grows fierce. You shall be my feast.”

The traveler pleaded, “Spare me. If you hunger, I offer you this endless bread, wine, and meat.”

Content with such sustenance, the giant accepted and shared the meal. Afterwards, the traveler inquired about the Golden Fortress. The giant searched his scrolls and maps but found none that named it. They waited for the giant’s brother, who returned with new charts. Still, the fortress lay unmarked—hidden far, far away.

Finally, the giant offered to carry the traveler near the fortress before he had to return to care for a child in his keeping. The traveler was set down near the distant mountain on which the fortress gleamed like a jewel, its walls shimmering as if forged from purest gold atop a mountain of polished crystal.

He tried to climb but the mountain’s smooth glass repelled him, and he fell again and again. Resigned, he built a simple hut by its base and watched the princess drive her carriage daily around her radiant home. Days turned to seasons, and one afternoon, he witnessed three brigands quarrelling fiercely nearby.

“Peace be upon you,” he called.

They paused and looked about but saw no one, resuming their fierce brawl.

“Peace be upon you,” he repeated twice more. At last, stepping forth, he asked the cause of their strife.

“I possess a rod that opens any door with a single tap,” said one.

“I have a cloak that renders its wearer invisible,” said another.

“My horse can leap over every pit and climb the glass mountain,” declared the third.

They debated whether to share these gifts or part ways.

“I will trade you for all three,” said the traveler, “not with coins but with a treasure of greater worth. First, let me test these wonders.”

Mounting the horse, donning the cloak, and grasping the rod, he became unseen and struck the brigands decisively with the rod, sending them fleeing in defeat.

Now unseen and mounted on swift horse, he scaled the crystal peak. At the fortress’s gate, locked tight, he tapped thrice with the rod; the door swung open as though by magic.

Inside, the princess sat alone, golden cup of wine before her. Still cloaked in invisibility, he slipped her ring from his finger into the cup. “That is my ring!” she exclaimed, “and the one who bears it must be near to free me!”

She searched throughout the fortress but found no one. When she reached the gate, the traveler threw off his cloak and revealed himself. Her joy was radiant as sunshine.

He embraced her gently, and she kissed him sweetly, saying, “You have truly freed me. On the morrow, we shall celebrate our union under the stars.”

Thus, the enchanted princess returned to her true form, and the traveler’s steadfast heart won both her freedom and her love in the timeless lands of Persia.
