HANSEL AND GRETEL

Near the vast, whispering woodlands that stretch across the ancient Persian countryside, there lived a humble woodcutter with his wife and their two children. The boy was named Bahram, and the girl, Laleh. In times of plenty, their modest life was sustained by the gifts of the forest, but when a cruel famine swept the land, the woodcutter found it impossible even to provide the daily crust of bread for his family.

One restless night, as Bahram lay troubled beneath the embroidered curtains of their small home, he shared his worries with his wife, Farzaneh. “What shall become of us?” he sighed. “How can we nourish our children when the granaries are empty, and we ourselves have naught to eat?”

With a cold resolve, Farzaneh answered, “Tomorrow, before dawn, we will take Bahram and Laleh deep into the forest, where the pines grow thick as the night itself. There, we shall build a fire, offer them a final piece of bread each, and leave them behind. They will not find their way home, and our burdens will lessen.”

Bahram’s heart clung to his children, but Farzaneh’s harsh words left him little choice. Only regret and sorrow filled his spirit.

The children, too, were restless in their beds. Laleh wept softly and whispered to Bahram, “Our fate is sealed.” But Bahram, determined, whispered back, “Fear not, sister, I will find a way.”

As moonlight streamed through the lattice windows, Bahram slipped quietly outside. The silver glow danced upon smooth white stones that gleamed like ancient pearls scattered on the garden path. Quietly, he gathered as many as the pockets of his cloak could hold.

The next morning, Farzaneh roused them, and they ventured deep into the forest under a pale dawn sky. Bahram dropped the gleaming pearls along the winding path, stealing glances back to the house, and telling his father he watched the morning sun kiss the silver rooftops, hidden as a playful white pigeon—a tale Farzaneh scoffed at.

When they reached a clearing, Farzaneh and the woodcutter lit a fire from fragrant cedar wood, and they told the children to rest while they went to gather wood anew. Bahram and Laleh tended the fire, nursing their small portions of bread as the chorus of woodpeckers and rustling leaves fooled them into believing their parents were near. When their eyes succumbed to exhaustion, sleep claimed them beneath a canopy of whispering cypress.

As darkness fell, Bahram awoke and took Laleh’s hand. Under the steady gaze of the full moon, the white pearls shimmered softly, guiding them through the labyrinth of shadows back to their home. Their father’s heart leapt at their return, and for a moment, joy lit the humble hearth.

Yet famine returned like a harsh desert wind. One night, Farzaneh spoke again to her husband with a chilling finality: “Only half a loaf remains. The children must be taken deeper into the forest, where they will not return.” The woodcutter’s heart broke, but he was overruled by his wife’s stern will.

This time, Bahram’s plan faltered. The door was locked, and he could not gather pearls. Instead, he crumbled their morsels of bread, hoping to mark a trail. But the forest’s many feathered dwellers carried away the crumbs, and when night fell, they were lost amidst ancient cedars and soaring planes.

For days they wandered this wild realm, faint and hungry, sustained only by handfuls of wild barberries and whispered prayers. On the third morning’s light, their ears caught the purest song—a nightingale perched upon a slender branch, singing melodies sweeter than the winds over the dunes.

Drawn by its enchantment, they followed the bird until it led them to a small cottage unlike any other. Its walls were baked from honeyed bread, and its windows gleamed with crystal sugar. “Let us feast here,” Bahram said, tasting a piece of the roof, while Laleh nibbled the sugared panes.

But a voice cracked through the air, ancient and heavy with malice: “Who dares nibble at my sweet dwelling?”

The children answered, “Only the desert wind, the blessed wind,” and pressed on with their feast.

Suddenly, the door creaked open, revealing an old woman leaning on a gnarled staff. Her eyes burned like embers beneath dark brows, and her smile was a shadow of kindness. She invited them inside, offering sweet dates, thick cream, and freshly baked saffron cakes. Exhausted, Bahram and Laleh surrendered to the comfort, believing themselves safe.

Yet beneath her tattered robes, the woman was a sorceress known for weaving cruel fates. She captured Bahram, locking him in a pen adorned with iron lattices, where she intended to fatten him for a dark feast. Laleh, forced to tend the fire and bring water, wept silently, praying to Anahita, goddess of mercy, for deliverance.

Days turned to weeks. Each morning the witch tested Bahram, but he deceived her with a slender bone disguised as his finger. Growing impatient, she declared the morrow would seal his doom.

When dawn came, the witch commanded Laleh to inspect the fiery oven’s heat, intending to trap the girl within its burning heart. But Laleh, quick and cunning, feigned ignorance and tricked the sorceress into climbing into the oven herself. With a mighty shove, she closed the iron door, and the witch’s terrible cries were swallowed by the flames.

Free at last, Bahram bounded from his cage, and together they gathered treasure—jewels, pearls, and ancient coins shimmering like the stars of Isfahan’s night sky. Laden with riches, they fled the witch’s forest.

Soon they reached a wide river, its waters deep and silent. No boat or bridge crossed its breadth. But on the sparkling surface floated a white duck, graceful as the moonlit swan of Persian legend. Laleh called softly:

“Oh white duck, shining bright,
Carry us across your silver light.”

The duck, a blessing from the river spirits, ferried them one by one to the far shore.

Beyond the river, the paths became familiar. At last, they sighted the low walls of their father’s home. Bursting inside, they embraced him, their joy mingled with grief for the absent Farzaneh, who had passed in her bitterness.

They emptied their sacks of pearls and treasures, and from that day forward, the woodcutter’s family knew a life of peace and plenty, under the watchful gaze of the eternal Persian sky.

And thus ends this tale, as the desert wind carries a small mouse past the window—whoever captures it shall find warmth in a fur hat as grand as any from the bazaars of Shiraz.
