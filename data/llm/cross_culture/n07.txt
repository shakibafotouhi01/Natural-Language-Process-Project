He of the Little Shell

Once upon a time, in a quiet Germanic realm cloaked by endless forests and misty moors, all the people had perished save for two helpless children—a baby boy and his elder sister. Their parents had died while they slept, leaving the siblings to awaken alone in a small timbered hut by the edge of the Schwarzwald.

The girl, a calm and watchful child, was the first to rouse. She looked around and, seeing no one but her little brother smiling in his dreams, lay back down in the straw-filled bed. Ten days passed, and the boy shifted slightly without opening his eyes. Ten days more, and he turned onto his other side, still sleeping peacefully. Yet in those quiet watches, a faint, silvery radiance hovered about his head, lighting the dark hut with an otherworldly glow that puzzled his sister.

As the seasons turned, the girl grew into a young woman; meanwhile, the boy’s growth was slow and measured. Many years went by before he could crawl, and well into his youth before he could stand unaided. When he finally learned to walk, his sister crafted a small bow and arrow for him, and hung a tiny snail shell around his neck, softly saying, “Your name shall be Schneckenkind—He of the Little Shell.”

Every day, the boy ventured into the dense woods with his bow, aiming at the small creatures of the forest. The first bird he felled was a lively coal tit. His sister was delighted and carefully preserved the delicate creature as a trophy. On the next day, he caught a red squirrel, which she also saved. By the third day, a wood partridge provided the evening meal.

With newfound courage, Schneckenkind roamed further into the forest. His skill grew as he hunted deer, boars, and even bears. Though small, he became a master woodsman, bringing back all he caught to share with his sister. Each time he entered their lodge, that same mysterious light danced about his head, filling the room with a strange, silver splendor.

Though Schneckenkind had reached the age of a man in years, he remained as small as a child. One winter’s day, wandering near a frozen lake, he spied a giant figure busy trapping beavers on the ice.

The giant was broad-shouldered, clad in a heavy fur coat lined with wolf pelts, and wielded a massive spear. Schneckenkind, no taller than a field mouse beside this colossus, settled on the shore to watch.

After the giant had hunted many beavers and hauled them onto a wooden sled, Schneckenkind crept alongside and, wielding his enchanted shell, deftly snipped off the tail of one beaver before slipping away. When the giant returned, he was baffled to find one of his catches missing its tail, muttering curses in his guttural voice.

The next day, Schneckenkind repeated the feat, again stealing a beaver’s tail from under the giant’s nose. Furious, the giant swore he would find and punish the elusive thief. Yet, despite his keen eyes and heavy footsteps, he found no trace—Schneckenkind’s magic made him invisible and silent as the forest shadows.

On the following day, the giant arrived early, hoping to trap the little man. Hunting the fresh tracks, he chased them through snow and ice, but just as he neared his hut, he caught sight of Schneckenkind—who allowed himself to be seen.

The giant growled thunderously, “Who are you, little man? I mean to vanquish you.”

But Schneckenkind replied with calm bravery, “You will never catch me.”

The giant lunged, but Schnneckenkind vanished like mist. “Where are you now?” demanded the giant.

“Under your belt,” came the voice. The giant struck his broad hand down, but found only empty air.

“Where next?” the giant roared.

“In your right nostril!” the voice teased. The giant grabbed at his nose, only to hear the tiny voice laughing far away on the snowy ground.

“Farewell, giant! Count your beaver tails—and know that I have taken one for my sister, too.”

As he vanished, the silver light returned, bathing the snow in a magical glow, a sight the slow-witted giant could not comprehend.

Returning home, Schneckenkind told his sister that their time together was nearing its end. “I must journey away; it is my fate. Tell me, where would you wish to dwell?”

“My heart longs for the place where dawn breaks—the eastern sky,” she said softly. “There, the first light of morn colors the clouds with red and gold, and I shall dwell among those rosy hues. When you see the painted skies, think of me, shining bright.”

“And I,” said Schneckenkind, “will make my home on the highest mountains, where streams run clear and the air is pure. There, golden light will shine forever around my head, and they will call me Berggeist—the Little Wild Man of the Mountains. But before we part, I must seek out the spirits who rule the earth and learn who will be friend or foe.”

So he traveled far across the land and even descended into the deep earth, where a fierce giant spirit kept a boiling cauldron. This giant, kin to the one who hunted beavers, recognized Schneckenkind and cast him into the kettle to drown.

But with the power of his magic shell, Schneckenkind emptied the cauldron in an instant and leapt free, unharmed.

He returned to his sister and told her all he had seen. “At each of the four corners of the earth dwells a spirit, and above them all a Great Being who decides our fate. There is also a wicked spirit deep beneath the earth to avoid. When the winds blow from the four corners, you must go—you will be carried to where you long to be. I shall dwell among the rocky heights, where my kin delight.”

With that, Schneckenkind took his bow and set out up a towering mountain. Along the path, a bright light shone around his head, and he sang a song to the winds:

Blow, winds, blow! My sister lingers  
Where the dawn with rosy fingers  
Paints the skies on morning’s breath,  
Bathing clouds in shades of death.  
There her gleaming face I’ll see,  
Guiding through the wood and lea.  
While I roam the mountain peaks,  
Sing my wild and wordless speaks.  

Soon the winds rose, swirling through the forest, lifting his sister toward the blush of dawn. There she remains still, a shining light in the morning sky—the Morning Star of the Schwarzwald, forever watching.
