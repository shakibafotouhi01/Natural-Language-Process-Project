The Celestial Sisters

In a secluded part of the Black Forest, where dense pines stretched endlessly and woodland creatures thrived, there dwelt a hunter named Weißfalke—the White Falcon. Renowned among his village for unmatched skill and fortune, he returned each evening bearing a generous spoil. His stature was as sturdy as the towering firs, his eyes flickered with youthful fire, and no shadowed glen or rugged trail eluded his keen sight.

One day, driven by restless curiosity, Weißfalke ventured farther than ever before, emerging into an open clearing that revealed a vast meadow stretching under a pale sky—the Landes, a sweeping plain where wild grasses swayed blue-gray and wildflowers gleamed like scattered jewels. As he wandered without a path, savoring the crisp air scented with pine resin and fresh earth, he stumbled upon a curious circle pressed into the meadow, a ring where the grass and blossoms seemed bent inward as if trod gently round and round. Yet no trace of footprints led to or from it—no crushed leaf nor snapped twig disturbed the perfection of the sod.

Perplexed, Weißfalke concealed himself nearby, watching intently. Soon, faint melodies, pure and haunting, wafted through the air. He lifted his gaze skyward, and as the music faded to a whisper, he beheld a small cloud-like basket descending gracefully. At first delicate as a wisp, it grew steadily larger until it revealed twelve sisters of radiant beauty, their forms as graceful as forest nymphs, their eyes bright as mountain lakes.

As their basket touched earth within the ring, they leapt out and commenced a lively dance, their feet striking a glowing orb that sang enchanting tunes, keeping rhythm with their joyous movements. Hidden in the shadows, Weißfalke watched spellbound, admiring them all; yet his heart was stolen by the youngest, whose laughter shimmered like the silver Danube.

Unable to remain a silent witness, he sprang forth to claim her, but at his approach, the sisters, swift as the forest birds, leapt back into their basket and were lifted away, disappearing into the misty heights.

Sorrow-filled, Weißfalke gazed long after the basket fading into clouds. “They are gone,” he sighed, “never to grace my sight again.” But longing would not let him rest.

The following day, at the same hour, he returned. To trick the sisters, he assumed the shape of a sable, blending into the underbrush near the enchanted ring, pretending to forage quietly. The basket appeared as before, the sweet melodies drawing near. Yet the moment the sisters glimpsed him, startled, they fled skyward in song.

“Perhaps they wish to test our mortal wiles,” mused the eldest sister as they hovered.

“Nay,” the youngest replied, “let us rise quickly.”

Their voices united in a chant, and the basket vanished among the clouds.

Heart heavy, Weißfalke shed his guise and returned alone to his forest cabin, the days ahead bitter and long without his beloved.

Determined, on the third day he sought a new stratagem. Near the ring he found an ancient, moss-covered tree stump, home to a family of field mice wanderers. Adopting the form of a mouse himself, he nestled among the little creatures, eyes sharp, ever watching the sky.

At the appointed time, the sisters descended as usual, resuming their dance and play. The youngest noticed the stump. “That was not here before!” she cried, fleeing toward the basket.

Her sisters smiled kindly and poked the stump, revealing the startled mice — among them Weißfalke. They swiftly caught all but one, who scampered wildly. The youngest pursued, raising a slender silver wand to strike the little creature, when suddenly the White Falcon stood, embracing the youngest sister in his arms. Eleven of the sisters fled upward in their basket, leaving the young one behind.

Weißfalke tended his bride with devoted care—wiping away her tears, recounting tales of hunting and earthbound delights. Together they walked past the whispering forest to his humble home, where joy blossomed like the springtime crocus, and their hearts beat in harmony.

Seasons turned—the cold winters and warm summers—and soon their happiness was crowned by a son, fair and strong, the light of stars mingled with earthly vigor.

But the celestial maiden longed for her father among the stars. Hidden from her husband, she fashioned a delicate woven basket and gathered rare forest treasures and sweetest food, planning a secret voyage home.

One day, with Weißfalke away hunting, she entered the enchanted ring with her child. Singing a mournful, haunting melody, she rose skyward in the shimmering basket. Though Weißfalke rushed to the meadow, he could not reach them before they vanished into the heavens. He cried out in vain as they became a twinkling light fading beyond the clouds. Grief overtook him; his world dimmed.

Winter and summer passed. Weißfalke mourned his lost wife and son, whose visage combined her light with his strength.

Meanwhile, in the starry realm, the maiden soon forgot her earthly sorrow amid her father’s celestial splendor. Yet her son grew restless, yearning to know his origins. The Star Chief spoke to his daughter:

“Go, child, and bring your son to his father, bidding him come join us. But tell him he must bring the gifts of all beasts and birds he encounters in his hunts.”

So they descended to earth, their voices borne on the crisp night wind. Weißfalke, ever near the enchanted ring, recognized them at once, embracing wife and son. He heeded the message and hunted with urgent zeal, collecting token feathers, tails, and paws of creatures rare and wondrous.

When all was ready, Weißfalke visited cherished places—the hilltops gazing east at dawn, the brook where he played as a youth, his lonely lodge now heavy with memory—and at last the enchanted ring. There, hand in hand with wife and son, he entered the glowing basket, rising beyond the reach of dark forests or mortal sight.

Upon arrival, the Star Chief welcomed all to a grand feast and proclaimed that each might choose to remain as they were or become one with the earthly gifts brought forth. A lively confusion ensued—some took paws, some wings, tails, or claws. Those choosing paws or claws transformed into animals, bounding away; others became birds, soaring skyward.

Weißfalke chose a white falcon’s feather. His wife and son followed, each transformed into a white falcon. Spreading their wings, they descended together to earth, their eyes reflecting the stars, their flight kissed by the freedom of celestial winds—forever guardians between sky and forest.
