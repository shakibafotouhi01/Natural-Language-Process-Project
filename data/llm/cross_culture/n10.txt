Strong Desire and the Red Sorcerer

Once upon a time, in the shadowy lands of the dense Bavarian forests, there lived a man named Starkes Verlangen—that is, Strong Desire—who had a wife and a young son. As winter's grip eased, Starkes Verlangen moved his family from their village nestled among timbered hills to the edge of a vast woodland where game was plentiful, a full day's journey from their village. Beneath the towering spruces, the wife busied herself setting up their humble hut, while the father ventured out to hunt.

One evening, as the sun was sinking behind the hills, Starkes Verlangen returned weary but triumphant, bearing a large deer. Thirsty and spent, he called his son to fetch water from a nearby brook. But the boy, though named Strong Desire, shook his head, saying the darkness frightened him. His father urged, reminding him that his mother and he were tired and the brook was but a short way off. Still, the boy would not budge.

“Ah, my son,” the father said, sadly, “if you fear to fetch water in the dark, you will never defeat the Red Sorcerer.”

These words struck deep into the boy’s heart like the jagged edge of a sword. He sat through the night by their hut’s wooden door, gazing upon the starry sky, sighing as one burdened with great sorrow.

At dawn, Strong Desire asked his mother to fashion moccasins from the deer’s hide while he prepared his bow and arrows with solemn purpose. Without a word to his parents, he left the cabin as the mist curled low over the forest floor. With resolve, he shot an arrow into the pale morning sky, letting it fall westward. Following its path, he found it piercing a stag’s heart. He feasted on venison, and the next day repeated the ritual. Four arrows flew, and each night, four deer lay dead by his hand.

In his haste and distress, he left the arrows lodged in the prey, and when the fifth dawn came, he was arrowless and hungry. Despairing, he collapsed on the cold earth, ready to give up. But then a low, rumbling sound stirred the forest beneath him, like the distant tremble of the earth.

Rising, he saw in the distance a figure—small and bent—walking with a wooden staff along a smooth path that led from a gloomy cabin to a dark, brackish lake whose black waters seemed bottomless. Surprised, Strong Desire crept closer and hid among the pines. The figure was none other than the dreaded Kriegsmagd—the War Witch—an ancient crone shrouded in mystery and fear. Her staff was adorned with a string of raven feathers and painted owl talons, which fluttered with every tap she made on the earth, sending forth eerie birdcalls.

The crone entered her cabin and cast off her mantle, woven entirely of women’s hair and detached locks, which when shaken, emitted cackles of laughter that chilled the soul. Though frightened, the boy spoke no word.

Then the witch fixed her sharp eyes upon him and said, “I have watched you from the moment you left your father’s lodge. Fear not, for I shall be your protector and guide.” She invited him within, offering supper and asking why he traveled so far.

Strong Desire told her of his shame and the hardships he faced after refusing to fetch water at night. The witch nodded slowly.

“Tell me truly," she said, "were you afraid to go to the brook in the dark?”

“I was,” he admitted.

With a gesture of her staff, the raven feathers shrieked and the mantle quaked, the hair emitting a dreadful, mocking laughter.

“And are you afraid now?” she asked again.

“I am,” he answered boldly.

“But you speak truth without hesitation. Courage lives in you yet,” the Kriegsmagd smiled. She took a heavy lead comb and drew it through his short hair again and again until it flowed like that of a maiden of the village. Then she clothed him in a woman’s gown embroidered with forest flowers and painted his cheeks a rosy red. She gave him a shining silver bowl and told him to carry a blade of scented sedge at his waist. “Tomorrow,” she said, “go to the edge of that lake. The Red Sorcerer rules there from his isle. Many men of the village will beg you to become their wife and take you to their homes. Refuse them all, and say you seek only the hand of the Red Sorcerer. When he comes for you, journey with him, but at night, lead him from the village to a lonely path, and then with the blade of sedge, strike his head from his shoulders.”

Though fear gnawed at his heart, the memory of his father’s disappointment steeled his will.

At dawn, he left the War Witch’s cabin, now swallowed in a dense, pale fog thick as wool, which soon vanished when he turned to look back.

He followed the worn path to the lake’s shore opposite the Red Sorcerer’s island. The morning was bright and clear, the sunlight shimmering on the water like polished glass.

He dipped the silver bowl into the lake, drawing it out glittering, and soon a flotilla of wooden canoes, carved with serpent heads and painted with runes, swept from the island. The islanders admired his delicate dress and fair beauty, pleading for marriage with earnest voices. Strong Desire refused each boldly.

When word reached the Red Sorcerer, he launched his grand boat, its ribs formed by living asp serpents with emerald eyes, their heads raised in grim guard. As Strong Desire stepped aboard, the snakes hissed and rattled fiercely, filling the air with dread, but the sorcerer calmed them with whispered spells.

The marriage was swift, held beneath the twisted branches of ancient yews. Strong Desire gave rich gifts from the War Witch's hoard.

But the Red Sorcerer’s mother, stern and sharp-eyed, gazed long upon her new daughter-in-law. Suspicion clouded her face—it was no woman but a man, she was sure. She warned her son, but he scolded her, breaking his pipe and calling her an old owl.

Shaken and offended, Strong Desire rose, declaring he could not remain after such insult, and left the lodge with the sorcerer trailing behind. They walked to the island’s beach where the waves whispered secrets to the shore.

Red Sorcerer begged him to stay, promising wealth and power, but Strong Desire was unmoved. Finally seated, the sorcerer rested his head in the lap of his strange bride, exhausted.

“Red Sorcerer,” the youth whispered softly, “you have slain many men.”

“Hundreds,” the sorcerer replied, a cruel grin creeping across his face. “Now, with this marriage, I will bring ruin to thousands more.”

“And you would kill even me if I were not your wife,” coaxed Strong Desire, with a sweet smile.

“Indeed,” said Red Sorcerer. “Sleep now, and dream of slaughter.”

Soon the sorcerer was fast asleep.

Seizing the moment, Strong Desire drew the blade of sedge in a swift, clean stroke, severing the wicked head from the shoulders. Casting off the gown that had concealed his true self, he grasped the bleeding head and plunged into the cold waters, swimming swiftly to the mainland.

Behind him, he heard the anguished cries of those who sought the newlyweds, finding only the lifeless, headless body.

The War Witch welcomed him back with laughter, praising his bravery and shrewdness. “You need not have taken the scalp,” she said, cutting a lock of hair for herself. “Now go home with this—proof that will earn your respect.”

She warned him that on the way he would face one last peril. The Earth Spirit Maunkahkeesh demands a sacrifice of all great heroes. When an earthquake splits the earth beneath, he must cast a partridge into the chasm and leap over it swiftly.

Grateful, Strong Desire followed these instructions and reached his village in safety.

His parents mourned, believing him lost, and many false sons appeared trying to claim his place. But when their true son stood before them, changed from a timid boy into a man of courage and deeds, tears filled their eyes.

Yet the young villagers laughed, mocking the once-fearful son.

Then, with solemn pride, Strong Desire revealed the ghastly trophy—the severed head of the Red Sorcerer, eyes still fixed in a fiendish leer.

The laughter ceased. Respect and awe replaced their scorn.

From that day, Strong Desire was hailed a hero, lifted among the bravest warriors of his people, and finally became a revered chieftain. His family, too, earned honor and esteem for generations to come.
