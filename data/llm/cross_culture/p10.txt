9  - Saiawosh

Long ago, beneath the endless sky and among the great forests of the plains, there came forth four noble warriors—Toos, Gew, Goodarz, and others—from the wide tribes of the land of the Eagle’s Nest. They ventured into the sacred groves of the Great Oak to chase the wild mustangs that roamed free. Deep within the woods, they found a woman of such rare beauty that even the whispering winds paused to admire her. Toos and Gew felt their hearts kindle like fire at her sight.

They asked of her roots and learned she came from the ancient line of Feridoun, a lineage honored among all tribes. Each warrior wished to claim her as his wife, but neither would step aside. Their voices rose like thunder, and they nearly came to blows. Then a wise elder spoke, “Let the Black Hawk, Kai Kawous, decide between you.” They agreed and journeyed to the great lodge of Kai Kawous, carrying the woman with them.

Kai Kawous gazed upon her radiant face and his own heart burned with desire. “She is worthy of a chief,” he declared, and he took her to live among his women. In time, she bore him a son, strong and tall like the towering cedars—he was named Saiawosh. Joy filled Kai Kawous’s heart, yet a shadow crossed it, for the stars had whispered a troubling prophecy: the heavens would challenge this child, and even his greatest strengths might not save him from fate.

News of the child’s birth traveled far, reaching the land of Rostam, the mighty warrior of the Bear Clan. Stirred from his mourning for a lost son, Rostam journeyed to the Eagle’s Nest, asking to raise Saiawosh as his own. Kai Kawous consented, and Rostam brought the boy to his village, teaching him the ways of the warrior and the customs of feasting under the open skies. Saiawosh grew in strength and honor, unmatched across the lands.

When Saiawosh was old enough to catch a great mountain lion with his own hands, he sought out Rostam and said, “It is time I meet the Black Hawk, my father, and show him the man I have become.” Rostam, proud, led the host through the rolling hills to Kai Kawous’s lodge. The tribes rejoiced at Saiawosh’s arrival, and Kai Kawous welcomed him with gifts of turquoise and silver, placing him beside himself on the council seat. For seven years, Saiawosh proved his worth, and in the eighth year, his father crowned him chief, and all believed the stars’ dark warnings were false.

But the weaving of fate is never so easily undone. Sudaveh, a woman at the lodge, saw Saiawosh’s youthful vigor and beauty, and her heart burned with secret longing. She sent whispers inviting him into the women’s quarters. Saiawosh, cautious and honorable, declined. Sudaveh’s envy grew, and she told Kai Kawous lies, accusing Saiawosh of disrespect and deception. Blinded by love for Sudaveh and distrust, Kai Kawous struggled between fatherly love and suspicion.

To test Saiawosh’s truth, Kai Kawous ordered a great fire to be built—a pyre towering like the sacred mountain, ablaze with cedar and pine, its smoke reaching the stars. The son was commanded to ride his horse through the flame. With a prayer to the Great Spirit, Saiawosh rode undaunted into the fire. All who watched held their breath, believing no man could survive such a trial.

But Saiawosh emerged unharmed, his robes clean, not a hair singed. A shout of joy echoed from valley to river. Sudaveh’s heart sank while the tribes praised Saiawosh’s bravery and truth. Kai Kawous embraced his son, and peace seemed restored. Yet Sudaveh’s shadow lingered, and her lies stirred again, poisoning his father’s heart. Though Saiawosh pleaded for peace, the seeds of doubt took root.

When war drums thundered and Afrasiyab, the Wolf King from the northern lands, gathered his fierce warriors to challenge the Eagle’s Nest, Saiawosh asked for the honor to lead the warriors. With Rostam by his side, he marched against foes from the distant Turan forests. The battles raged like summer storms; at last, Saiawosh’s forces triumphed, and he sent word to Kai Kawous of their success.

The Wolf King, hearing of the defeat, was troubled and dreamed of serpents besetting his lands—omens of his downfall at the hands of Saiawosh. Seeking peace, Afrasiyab sent gifts and ambassadors, asking Saiawosh to lay down arms and make a pact. Saiawosh and Rostam agreed to parley, but with caution, demanding hostages to ensure trust.

A fragile peace was forged, yet Kai Kawous, hearing of these dealings, was filled with rage and scorned Saiawosh for what he saw as weakness. He commanded Saiawosh to break the peace and destroy the hostages and tribes beyond the borders. But Saiawosh, bound by honor and the spirits’ guidance, refused.

Choosing exile over betrayal, Saiawosh sent back the hostages and sought refuge in the lands of Afrasiyab. The Wolf King, advised by the wise Piran, welcomed Saiawosh as kin, giving him a wife, Ferangis, Afrasiyab’s daughter, and a throne within Turan. Saiawosh built a city named Gangdis amid the plains—a place of beauty and song, where the people rejoiced in games and dances beneath the sun.

Yet fate’s shadow crept closer. Jealousy poisoned Afrasiyab’s brother Gersiwaz, who plotted against Saiawosh. Feigning friendship, Gersiwaz sowed distrust, whispering to Afrasiyab that Saiawosh planned betrayal. Afrasiyab’s anger burned, and soon he marched against Saiawosh’s city.

Forewarned, Saiawosh spoke with Ferangis, preparing her for the coming loss. With tears and courage, he mounted his horse and faced the army of Turan unarmed, refusing to bite the hand that fed him. His warriors fell like autumn leaves around him, and Saiawosh was taken captive.

Though Afrasiyab’s heart wavered, influenced by Gersiwaz’s poison, Ferangis stood before her father, begging for Saiawosh’s life and warning of the doom that killing him would bring. Yet rage ruled Afrasiyab’s spirit, and Saiawosh was slain.

As the blade fell, storms raged—heavens wept, and the earth trembled, mourning the fall of a noble chief. Realizing too late, Afrasiyab confined Ferangis to the shadows of the lodge. Piran pleaded for her life, offering to protect her far from the reach of pain.

In the quiet that followed, Afrasiyab’s heart burned with sorrow and regret. The spirit of Saiawosh lived on in stories told beneath the stars—of honor, betrayal, and the weaving of fate that no power could unravel beneath the endless sky.
