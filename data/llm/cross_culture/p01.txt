The faith of a starving man

Long ago, there was a humble wanderer, a man of deep spirit and strong heart, journeying across the vast and endless plains under the great sky. Hunger gnawed at him, and he sought sustenance among the whispering winds and ancient trails. As he walked along a worn path through the tall grasses, he came upon an empty pouch that had been left beside the trail by another traveler. He lifted the pouch, draped it over his shoulder, and with a voice carried by the breeze, he gave thanks: “Great Spirit, I am grateful to receive this empty pouch, though hungry, it holds the promise of something more.”

Further along, the wanderer found a cracked bow lying beneath a sacred pine, its string broken and useless for hunting. He gathered it carefully and placed it in his pouch, offering another prayer: “Great Spirit, I give thanks for this bow without a string, for it is a tool waiting for your guidance.”

After some time, he came to the remains of a withered tree, its limbs bare and lifeless. From its dry branches, he broke a few and tucked them inside his pouch, lifting his voice again to the sky: “Great Spirit, I honor you for showing me this old tree, whose branches will warm my fire.”

As he continued, he found an old clay pot, cracked and worn, dust settling upon its surface like the earth’s own breath. He brushed it clean with care, placed it in his pouch, and spoke with gratitude: “Great Spirit, thank you for this pot, cracked yet ready to hold the gift of life.”

Near a winding creek, a single fishing hook lay abandoned on the shore, alone without its line. The wanderer took the hook, placed it in his pouch, and with reverence, he spoke: “Great Spirit, thank you for this hook, small but hopeful.”

At last, the journey led him to the wide, flowing river—its waters stretching beyond sight, deep and endless, beyond his crossing. There he knelt upon the earth and gave thanks: “Great Spirit, I am grateful for this mighty river, a barrier and a blessing.”

With steady hands, the wanderer tied the fishing hook to the broken bowstring, fashioning a new tool by the power of his faith and patience. He cast it into the river and soon drew forth a shining fish. Using the dry branches from the withered tree, he kindled a warm fire, and in the cracked pot he cooked the fish, partaking in the sacred gift the Great Spirit had woven for him on his path.

Thus, the humble wanderer’s faith and gratitude transformed what seemed like emptiness and brokenness into nourishment and hope, guided always by the spirit of the land and sky.
