LILY AND THE LION

In a bustling Persian bazaar, there once lived a wealthy merchant with three daughters. Before he embarked on a long journey through the sunlit deserts and fragrant gardens of Shiraz, he asked each daughter what gift she would desire upon his return. The eldest wished for pearls from the Persian Gulf, the second for sparkling gems mined from the mountains near Isfahan, but the youngest, named Gulrukh (meaning ‘Face like a rose’), simply requested a single rose from the royal gardens of Kashan. Though winter’s chill made such a flower impossible, her father, loving her above all, vowed to try his best.

When his journey ended, the merchant returned with precious pearls and jewels for his two elder daughters, but finding a rose proved hopeless. In every garden, he was told sternly, “Roses do not bloom beneath snow.” As he traveled sadly toward home through the fragrant whispers of softly falling snow, he came upon a splendid palace surrounded by a strange garden. Half of the garden flourished in fiery spring blossoms, and the other half lay frozen beneath winter’s white cloak.

“Fortune smiles upon me,” he said, beckoning his servant to gather a rose from the blooming half. Yet, as they rode away with the coveted flower, a great and fearsome lion leapt from the shadows. With a voice like thunder across the desert, he thundered, “Who steals my roses must be devoured by my jaws!” Begging for mercy, the merchant pleaded, but the lion declared, “I will spare your life only if you promise to give me whatever greets you first upon your return.” At first, the merchant thought it might be a stray dog or a village cat, and with a heavy heart accepted the lion’s demand.

But as he neared his home, it was Gulrukh herself, radiant and joyful, who ran to embrace him, kissing his weathered face. Stricken with sorrow, the merchant revealed the terrible pact he had made and begged her to stay away from the lion. But Gulrukh, brave and steadfast, said, “Father, your word is sacred. I will go to the lion, and with kindness tame his heart. Perhaps he will let me return.”

At dawn, she ventured forth into the enchanted wood. The lion, she soon learned, was a prince under a powerful spell: by day, a great lion, and by night, a noble man. Welcoming her to his crystal palace, he treated her with such grace that she agreed to marry him. Their wedding feast was joyous, and they lived happily, though the prince vanished each morning to mysterious places and returned at nightfall.

One day, the prince told her that her eldest sister’s wedding feast would be held soon at their father’s home and offered to lead her there with his lion attendants. Overjoyed, Gulrukh returned, bringing light and laughter to her family long believing her lost. Soon after, her second sister also married, and when Gulrukh wished to attend, she implored her husband to accompany her. With grave fear, he explained that even the flicker of a single torchlight might deepen his enchantment, turning him into a dove doomed to wander the earth for seven years.

Despite his warnings, Gulrukh insisted, assuring him that she would shield him from all light. They journeyed together, bringing their small child, and the prince waited secluded in a thick-walled chamber during the evening’s celebrations. But through a crack in the door, a sliver of flame drifted unknowingly upon him, and in an instant, the prince vanished—replaced by a white dove who whispered, “For seven years I must fly the earth’s face, leaving white feathers as signposts on the winds. Follow them, and perhaps you shall find me again.”

So began Gulrukh’s quest across the vast lands of Persia. For seven long years, she followed the white feathers north and south, over rugged mountains and fertile plains, never resting. One day, the feathers ceased to fall, and the dove was gone. Desperately, she called to the great sun, its golden disc kindling the horizons. “Have you seen my dove?” she implored. The sun replied, “No, but take this jewel box, and open it when your need is greatest.” At night, under the silver gaze of the moon, she asked the same, and it gave her a delicate gilded egg. Then the desert winds, swirling through ancient cypresses, carried her voice, promising guidance and warning her to seek the fierce south wind.

The south wind spoke, “The dove has fled to the Red Sea, where the seven years have ended, and the lion battles a monstrous dragon—an enchantress who seeks to tear you apart. Find the rods by the sea; breaking the eleventh will defeat the dragon and free your prince. Then mount the mighty simurgh—a winged creature of legend—to carry you home. But beware: at sea’s midpoint, drop this nut to grow a tree for the simurgh’s rest. Forget, and the waters will claim you.”

Steeled with resolve, Gulrukh journeyed to the burning shores, found the rods, and struck the dragon with the eleventh. The lion was restored to princely form, the dragon to a princess once more. But no sooner was the spell broken than the princess seized the prince and fled on the simurgh’s back. Alone once more, Gulrukh vowed to search as long as the winds blew and the roosters crowed.

At last, she found the palace where the princess planned the prince’s wedding. Wearing the dazzling cloak from the sun’s box, she entered and offered it in exchange for a moment with her husband. The princess agreed but drugged the prince to keep him asleep. Though Gulrukh’s voice poured her sorrows and loyalty at his feet, he drifted like a dream-caught reed among the cypresses.

Left heartbroken, Gulrukh wandered into a meadow. Remembering the moon’s gift, she broke the golden egg—and out sprang a mother hen and twelve golden chicks that danced like flickering flames. Seeing this marvel, the princess again offered a bargain, and with a heavy mind, Gulrukh returned to the prince.

But this time, the prince, warned by his faithful chamberlain, kept his senses and recognized Gulrukh’s voice. Awaking, he embraced her, weeping, “You are my awakening from a deep spell.”

Together, they slipped away under moonlight onto the simurgh’s back. Halfway across the sapphire waters of the Red Sea, Gulrukh cast down the nut, raising a towering tree for the weary simurgh. Safely ashore, they found their child grown to beauty and strength.

Thus, after trials as fierce as the desert sun and as enduring as the ancient mountains, Gulrukh and her prince lived in joy and harmony, their love a tale whispered beneath Persian stars for generations to come.
