Gulistan -chapter III- story 28

Long ago, in a village beside a great river, there lived a young warrior who had fallen into hardship. His spirit was strong, but his hands were empty. The hunger in his heart grew, and he asked his father’s blessing to leave the lodge and travel, hoping to find a living by the power of his own arm.

The father, wise with years like the ancient trees, said: “Son, place the feet of contentment beneath the blanket of safety. The elders have spoken that joy is not born from endless struggle, and the greatest medicine for want is to hold one’s desires lightly.

No one seizes fortune like snaring the wind.
No arrows pierce the sky with empty hands.

If you carry a hundred skills, but the spirits do not favor you,
Your strength will be no shield against hardship.

What can a warrior do against a cruel wind?
The hand of fate is stronger than the hand of a bear.”

The son answered: “Father, traveling brings many gifts—renewing the spirit, learning the songs of other tribes, meeting friends, gaining honor, and seeing the world’s great wonders. The journey shapes a man, as the traveling shamans teach:

If you never leave your fire circle,
You will never become the eagle who soars.
Walk the land far and wide,
Before you walk the final path.”

The father nodded and replied, “The gifts of travel bless only a few: first, the wealthy trader whose tents rise in every village, surrounded by helpers and guarded by strong warriors. Wherever he travels, he is honored.

A man rich in buffalo hides travels safely through mountain and desert.
But the poor man is a stranger even in his own village.

Second, the wise elder, who draws crowds with his words, whose voice carries the wisdom of the ancestors. The people honor such a presence like shining obsidian.

A learned man is like the sun on the morning dew,
Seen and treasured wherever he treads.
But a fool, even if born a chief’s son,
Is like a black crow lost among eagles.

Third, the handsome warrior whose face holds the beauty of spring’s first bloom, who opens the hearts of others like the sacred door of a lodge.

Beauty walks wrapped in honor,
Even when the father’s fire cools.
A warrior's face is a gift to all who meet him,
A precious shell sought by many.

Fourth, the singer whose voice flows like the river in spring, whose songs sway even the wildest hearts, bringing peace to the campfires at night.

Sweet songs are medicine to the soul,
Bringing joy that no blade can cut.
Better than a strong arm is a gentle voice,
Feeding the spirit as spring feeds the earth.

Fifth, the craftsman who earns his place by his hands, his work seen and respected, so that his spirit is not lost as he follows his journey.

A cloak-maker travels without fear,
For his skill and patience provide.
But when the great chief falls,
Even kings sleep hungry.

These gifts bring joy on the road, but you, son, who have none, venture out with only daydreams. The world turns harshly, and the winds do not soften for those without the right paths.”

The son spoke once more: “Father, the wise say fate brings food and hardship alike, yet men must seek their path and stand against the storm. Though the night may hold danger, a brave heart must dare the journey when trapped by hunger and sorrow.

When a man has fallen from honor,
Why should he suffer still more pain?
The whole horizon is his home,
And the night shelters all travelers—rich and poor alike.”

With these words, the son bade his father farewell and began his journey alone. He said quietly, “When luck turns its face away, a skilled man must go where no one knows his story.”

He traveled until he reached the wild river, its waters roaring like thunder and tossing stones like warriors clashing. At the crossing, many sat with coins in hand, waiting for passage. The young warrior spoke humbly, asking to cross, but those gathered turned away.

“No one crosses without tribute,
No strength can move the silent hand of gold.”

A boatman laughed harshly, “No money—no crossing! What use is the strength of ten men? Pay the price.”

Anger flared in the warrior’s heart. As the boat pulled away, he cried out, “If my robe can buy the way, take it and bring me across.” The boat turned back, greed overcoming pride.

Desire blinds the clever,
Greed tightens the hunter’s snare.

When the warrior reached, he seized the boatman’s collar and brought him down. Another tried to help but met the same fate and fled. The crowd yield, bowing to the young man, who was then carried gently across the river.

“Where there is strife, be gentle,
A soft word can lead the fiercest heart.
Lead the bear by a single hair,
Not by force that breaks the willow.”

The boat reached a lone stone pillar standing midstream, and the boatman warned, “The boat must be tied or lost. Whoever is strongest, climb the pillar to fasten the cord.”

Proud and strong, the young warrior climbed. But as he grasped the rope, the boatman let it slip and pushed the vessel away, leaving the warrior stranded. Days passed; hunger and thirst chased his footsteps. He sank into the river under the weight of exhaustion, but the Great Spirit spared him.

He awoke on the far shore, weak and wild-eyed, eating roots and leaves to live. He wandered the desert until he found a well, where people drank and laughed. He begged for a coin but was turned away. Frustrated, he scolded and fought those who mocked him, but the many were too strong, and he fell wounded.

Even the mightiest bear
Can be overcome by the swarm of ants.
When small creatures unite,
They strip the fierce lion's hide.

Weak and alone, he joined a traveling band moving toward the night, but thieves lurked nearby, and the journey held danger. The warrior boasted of his strength, declaring he could face fifty foes alone. The others welcomed him warmly, bringing food and water. But when hunger blessed him with sleep, a wise elder whispered caution:

“This guard may bring more harm than the thieves.
Once a stranger’s friend stole his gold,
Leaving him naked and weeping at dawn.”

“Let us leave this stranger behind,” the others whispered, afraid and suspicious. They rose with the sun and departed, abandoning the sleeping warrior.

Lost and parched, the warrior sat with his heart breaking. “Who will stand with me once the trading horses have gone? A stranger walks with only a stranger’s shadow.”

He spoke these words when a prince from a distant tribe hunting nearby found him. Seeing his noble bearing beneath sorrow’s veil, the prince asked his story. Moved by his plight, the prince clothed him in fine robes, gave him precious beads, and sent a trusted guide to lead him home.

When they returned, his father rejoiced, grateful for his safe return. That evening, the son told of the harsh boatman, the cruel desert people, and the betrayals along the path.

“My son,” the father said, “Have I not told you that the hands of the empty are like a broken bear’s paw?”

The son replied, “Father, no treasure comes without trial. No victory without risking the hunter’s life. I have suffered storms, but from the clouds I gathered honey. The wise say, fate sets the table, but man must bring the fire.”

“To fear the crocodile,
Is to never dive for pearls.

The stone beneath the grinding mill never moves,
Yet must bear the heaviest load.

What will the lion eat in his den?
What nourishment for the fallen hawk?

If you wish to catch game in your own lands,
You must have the hands and feet of the spider.”

The father nodded, “This time, the spirits favored you, and a royal hand healed your wounds. Such gifts are rare as the morning star’s dance.”

The story is like the time a great chief placed a precious eagle feather on the village totem, promising honor to any who could shoot an arrow through its eye. Many tried and missed, except a small boy, who at play let loose a random arrow, and it passed through perfectly. The boy earned the feather and gifts, but then, burning his bow and arrows, said, “Only the first light should last forever.”

The wise and the foolish, the strong and the weak—all walk the long trail, seeking their place beneath the sky.
