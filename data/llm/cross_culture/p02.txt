Gulistan - story 16

A companion of mine lamented the harsh times we lived in. He spoke of his meager resources and the weight of his large family, feeling too weak to carry the burden of hardship. He confessed that often he dreamed of traveling far beyond the mountains, to a land where no one would know his fortune or misfortune, so that whether he prospered or struggled, he could walk without judgment.

He said many wanderers roam hungry, unnoticed and forgotten.
Many lay at death’s door, and no one sheds a tear for them.

His heart was troubled, fearing the hidden whispers of rivals who mocked him unseen. They said he lacked the courage to endure hardship and would soon abandon his family, choosing comfort over honor:
“See him, that man without pride,
Never blessed by fortune’s smile,
Fleeing warmth, leaving kin to cold,
A shadow without a path home.”

Knowing I had some skill with numbers, he asked if I might use my influence with the tribal council to find him a position. This, he believed, would ease his mind and bind him with gratitude to me for life. I answered, “Friend, serving the chief comes with two faces: hope for sustenance, but danger to your spirit. Wise ones say it is folly to brave great risk for uncertain reward.”

No one visits the healer's lodge to seek wealth or claim the harvest’s fruits.
You must either bear your sorrow, or let your beloved children be scattered like leaves upon the wind.

He pressed, citing old teachings: “Have you not heard, ‘The one who betrays must tremble when judged by the Great Spirit’?”

I replied, “The path of truth is accepted by the Creator; no traveler is lost upon it. Wise elders say four fears haunt four souls: the outlaw fears the warrior, the thief fears the watchman, the unfaithful fears the witness, and the wanderer fears the guardian of law. But what can harm the heart that holds no guilt?”

“Do not spend beyond your means while in favor, lest when the winds turn, your foes mock your fall.
Be pure, brother, and fear no shadow.
Only the dirty are beaten by the stone’s edge.”

To soften his worry, I told the story of the fox fleeing in terror, fearing camels forced into service. His friends mocked: “What harm have you, fox, with beasts of burden?” The fox answered, “Silence. If the jealous say I am a camel and catch me, who else will help? The one bitten by the serpent dies before the bitter herb reaches him.”

“You are honorable and true, yet enemies wait in every shadow. If they speak ill of you, the chief’s judgement will fall heavy, and none will speak for you. Better to seek comfort and let go of dreams that weigh heavily. The elders say:

‘The river holds many treasures,
But safety lies upon the riverbank.’”

Hearing this, my friend’s face darkened. He rebuked me harshly, saying, “What wisdom is this? Philosophers are right: friends show their teeth at the feast but turn to silence in the cell.”

“Trust not the one who knocks upon fortune’s door,
Who calls himself kin but turns away in hardship.
True friendship is the hand that holds tight in the coldest night.”

Though hurt by his words, I sought the tribal chief. Calling on old bonds, I spoke of my friend’s plight. The chief granted him a small role, and through kindness and skill, my friend rose in esteem and rank, becoming a trusted advisor to the leader, his star ascending like the sun at dawn. I rejoiced, saying:

“Fear not tangled paths or shattered hearts,
For from darkness springs the life-giving light.
Grieve not, brother, for the Great Spirit hides blessings in the unseen.
Sit not in gloom for the changing winds,
For patience, though bitter, yields sweet fruit.”

On a journey with friends to the sacred mountain, I returned to find him waiting two day’s travel away, dressed like the wandering medicine men. His face was heavy with sorrow. When I asked, he told me:

“As you saw, envy found me and enemies brought false charges of betrayal. The chief did not seek the truth, and old friends fell silent, forgetting all we shared.

“See how those who praise the mighty stand with bowed heads—
But when fortune turns, all seek to trample them beneath their feet.”

“At last, after much suffering, news came that the sacred travelers approached, and I was freed, though my family’s lands were taken.”

I said, “You did not heed when I cautioned that service to chiefs is a voyage on a stormy lake—gain or death are the only outcomes.”

The traveler may bring the treasure ashore,
Or be found lifeless on the shoreline.

To soothe his wounds, I spoke gently:

“Did you not know your hands would be bound,
If you did not heed the counsel of your elders?

And if you cannot bear the sting’s pain,
Do not place your fingers where the scorpion dwells.”
