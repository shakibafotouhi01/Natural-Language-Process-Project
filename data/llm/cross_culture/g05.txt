RUMPELSTILTSKIN

In a distant land of ancient Persia, beside a sparkling river that wound through fragrant orchards and towering cypresses, stood a humble watermill. Near the mill dwelt a miller and his only daughter, a maiden of rare beauty and wit. The miller, proud beyond measure, boasted to the wandering Shah—who often came to the forest to hunt—that his daughter possessed the wondrous gift of transforming mere straw into pure gold.

The Shah, a man consumed by his desire for wealth, summoned the girl to his splendid palace of marble and turquoise tiles. There, in a grand chamber filled with heaps of dry straw, he placed before her an ornate spinning wheel of polished rosewood and commanded, “By the dawn’s first light, spin this straw into gold, or face the wrath of my displeasure.”

The poor maiden trembled and implored, “O great Shah, this is but my father's vain boast; I know nothing of such magic.” Yet the chamber’s heavy doors were sealed tightly, and she was left alone.

With a heart weighed by sorrow, she slumped into a cushioned corner, her tears falling like pearls upon the Persian carpet. Suddenly, the door creaked open and in limped a curious little figure, dressed in patched silks with eyes bright as stars—a creature of the jinn or perhaps a dervish of the unseen realm.

“Peace be upon you, fair daughter of the mill,” he said. “Why weep you so bitterly?” “Alas, I must spin this straw into gold, yet my hands know not how.”

The sprite smiled with a cunning glint. “Tell me what treasure you will offer me for this deed.”

“My necklace of carnelian beads,” she answered, hesitant but desperate.

Seizing the necklace, the little man turned to the wheel. He chanted softly in an ancient tongue and began to whirl the spindle:

“Turn, turn, O sacred thread,
From humble straw to gleaming dread,
By moon’s light and morning’s gleam,
Spin the gold from this poor dream.”

The wheel spun faster and faster, until the straw gleamed like the setting sun on the desert dunes.

When the Shah beheld the golden bounty, his eyes gleamed with greed anew. Yet he demanded the maiden spin a greater pile before the next nightfall. Again, trapped in her gilded prison, she cried out until the mysterious visitor returned. “What will you give me this time?”

“My mother’s ring,” she said, trembling.

Again, with whispered words and graceful spinning, the little man worked his marvel; the heaps became gold before dawn.

But the Shah’s hunger for riches was insatiable. He led her to a mountain of straw larger than before and said, “Spin all this into gold by sunrise, and you shall be my queen.”

Alone within the vast chamber, despair overcame her. The sprite appeared once more. “What can you offer now?”

“Nothing remains,” she sighed.

“Then promise me your firstborn child when you reign as queen,” the little man said with sharp eyes.

Though her heart quailed, she saw no choice and agreed.

Once more the wheel sang its spell, and the straw turned to gold beneath the watchful eye of dawn. The Shah, true to his word, married the miller’s daughter, who ascended the throne and wore a crown of emeralds and pearls.

When her first child was born, joy filled the palace halls, and she forgot the dark promise and the strange man. But one day, as she cradled her infant by the light of the quince tree, the little figure returned, demanding his due.

Grief-stricken, the queen offered all the wealth of her kingdom for release, but the stranger’s heart was hard. Then he spoke, “I grant you three days. If before they pass you can guess my name, you shall keep your child.”

Through sleepless nights she pondered names from far and near—names whispered along caravan routes and sung in taverns from Isfahan to Shiraz. She sent swift riders across the realm to discover every strange and secret name.

On the first day, she guessed names of noblemen and ancient poets: “Darius, Kaveh, Rostam.” But the stranger shook his head each time.

On the second, she tried playful names she had heard from children: “Kolah Namadi, Barfi, Shirazi." Yet again, the little man denied them all.

On the third day, a messenger returned from a lonely hill where peacocks roamed and pomegranate trees blossomed under a sapphire sky. The rider told her:

“As I climbed the hill at dusk, I saw a small hut where a fire flickered, and around it danced a peculiar little man on one leg, singing in a voice as light as jasmine breeze:

‘Joyfully I feast and play,
Brew today and bake next day;
Dance and chant without a shame,
For my name is Amoo Zhalam!’

The queen’s eyes brightened with hope. When the little man came, she smiled and called out, “Is your name Dervish?”

“No, O Queen!” came the sharp reply.

“Is it Bahram?”

“No, my lady!”

“Is it Amoo Zhalam?”

At this, the creature’s face twisted in fury. “A witch must have told you that!” he hissed, stamping a foot so hard it struck the stone floor, then clutching it fiercely to pull it free.

And with that, he vanished into the swirling night air. The nurse laughed softly, the baby gurgled sweetly, and the court burst into joyful mirth, exclaiming, “Long live the queen and her cleverness! May Amoo Zhalam’s name be forever known in tale and song.”
