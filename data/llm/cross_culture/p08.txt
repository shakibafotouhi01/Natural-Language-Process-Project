Rostam and Sohrab

Hear now the tale of Hawk and Mountain, a story told by the firesides beneath the vast sky, a sorrowful song of father and son set upon the great plains and towering forests of the First Peoples.

One morning, Mountain, a mighty warrior of the Eagle Clan, rose from his lodge with a heavy heart, sensing shadows in his spirit. To ease his mind, he saddled his wild stallion, Swiftwind, and set out toward the rugged lands that border the great river, near the village of the Bear Tribe. As he rode, he stirred a herd of wild deer, chasing them through the tall grasses until twilight embraced the land. He caught one, roasted it over flames, and rested by its warmth, while Swiftwind grazed quietly nearby.

While Mountain slept, seven warriors from a rival tribe came upon the horse, admiring its strength. They cast their ropes, seeking to take Swiftwind away. But the stallion, fierce as a thunderbird, struck back, breaking free by trampling one foe and scattering the others. Yet, outnumbered, Swiftwind was captured and taken to the enemy village.

Mountain awoke to find Swiftwind gone and was grieved, for the bond between horse and rider is sacred. Following the hoofprints, he arrived at the village gates. The chief and elders welcomed Mountain with respect, hearing his tale of loss. The chief swore no harm would come to the great warrior and offered him shelter while the search for Swiftwind continued.

At night, a radiant woman entered Mountain’s lodge, her movements like the gentle wind through cedar boughs, her robe scented with sweetgrass. She spoke softly, “I am Morningstar, daughter of the Bear Tribe chief. My heart has long yearned after your courage and strength. None among my people can match your valor. If you will listen, I shall bring Swiftwind to you, and offer my hand in friendship.”

Mountain saw wisdom in her eyes and beauty in her voice, and a hope grew in his heart. He sent a message to the chief, who gladly consented to their union. Soon, Mountain and Morningstar were joined beneath the watchful eyes of stars and earth, pledging to bear a child strong as the bear and swift as the eagle.

Before parting, Mountain gave Morningstar a small stone, black as night yet glowing softly—a token for their child. “Should a daughter be born, bind it among her braids to guard her; should it be a son, tie it to his arm that he may carry strength like his father.” Then swift as the wind, Mountain rode away with Swiftwind, leaving Morningstar behind, her heart full of both joy and sorrow.

Nine moons passed, and Morningstar bore a son, whom she named Hawk. From his first breaths, he smiled like the dawn. His limbs grew swift and strong as a river current, and by ten winters he outshone all in the village in skill and strength. One day, filled with fire, Hawk asked his mother, “Tell me of my father, whose blood runs through me, whose steps I follow. If you keep silence, I shall search the earth till I find him.”

Morningstar showed Hawk the token stone and spoke gently, “You are Mountain’s seed, born of courage and honor. But speak our secret softly, for the rival tribes watch for weakness. If they knew who you are, danger would fall upon you both. Your path is yours to choose, but your destiny is bound with Mountain’s.”

Hawk, burning with longing and pride, vowed to gather warriors and reclaim the land from those who oppose his people, dreaming of glory and the crown of his people. But first, he needed a horse, swift and strong like his spirit.

For days he sought among the herds until he learned of a foal descended from Swiftwind himself. Upon riding this foal, he declared, “With such a steed, I shall darken the skies for many foes.” Thus arrayed, Hawk gathered his warriors and set forth toward the lands of the Eagle Clan.

Word came to the enemy chief, Skyshadow, of Hawk’s growing force and fiery heart. Skyshadow plotted in darkness, sending spies and gifts to deceive the young warrior, while secretly preparing to exploit the secret of his birth. “If Hawk’s father learns who stands against him, the land will crumble. Better it be kept hidden that father and son face each other in battle.”

Hawk pressed onward, his path marked by the smoking ruins of camps, until he reached the White Lodge, heart of his father’s people. Its aged guardian, Oakshield, stood firm but was no match for Hawk’s youthful strength, and was taken captive.

Then came Oakshield’s daughter, Riverwind, fierce as a storm. She donned armor, hid her hair beneath a warrior’s helmet, and rode forth challenging Hawk to combat. None dared face her, mistaking her for a mighty chief, but Hawk accepted her challenge boldly. Their battle was thunderous—arrows flew like lightning, lances clashed like crashing waves, but Hawk’s power prevailed. Yet when he lifted her helmet, she was revealed as a woman, and he marveled at her strength.

Seeking peace, Riverwind offered the White Lodge and its treasures if Hawk would end the fight. Swayed by her words and beauty, he followed her inside, but she betrayed him, locking the doors and mocking him from the battlements. Incensed, Hawk vowed vengeance and rode forth, leaving the lodge a shadow of broken promises.

Meanwhile, Oakshield sent word to Mountain’s people of the coming storm—a youthful chief who fought like the gods themselves. The elders called upon Mountain to return and defend his people.

Mountain answered the call, but the chieftain Kaiwin, lord of the people, greeted him with scorn and harsh words, even threatening his honor. Mountain’s pride burned, and he withdrew from the court, yet was begged by the elders to stand with his people.

As Mountain prepared, he secretly ventured to Hawk’s camp by night, cloaked among the enemy. There he saw his son—a tall, proud youth surrounded by loyal warriors, feasting and laughing unaware of the fate that would bind them. In the shadows, Mountain’s brother-in-law, Silent River, moved to confront the stranger and was struck down by Mountain’s hand.

Hawk lamented the loss but kept his feast, unwilling to dampen the spirits of his warriors.

Morning dawned, and Hawk questioned the captives within the White Lodge about the war camps, seeking the identity of his father. The answers came with caution or silence, for none wished to reveal the secret that might lead to tragedy.

Frustrated, Hawk struck the elder Oakshield down but was no closer to the truth.

In time, Hawk and Mountain met on the open plain between their armies. Mountain begged the youth to abandon the fight, fearing the blood that might be spilled between kin. Hawk, burning with hope, asked Mountain to tell him his name, believing the war-chief before him to be his father. Mountain denied, speaking words to unsettle his son’s heart.

Their battle was fierce — clubs hammered like thunder, spears shattered like dry wood, swords rang like thunderclaps. Day faded to night, yet neither yielded, their strength and skill matched. Both men bore wounds yet fought on, each the other’s equal, and Mountain’s heart was heavy with sorrow.

At last, Hawk knelt upon Mountain, sword raised to end the contest. Mountain spoke swiftly, “In our people’s law, one who downstrikes first must spare his foe for another fight.” Hawk stayed his hand, and they paused in the battle to catch breath.

But the war stirred again the next day. Hawk, fierce as the storm, charged with renewed fury, and Mountain, steeled by spirit and prayer to the Great Spirit, met him blow for blow. At last, Mountain overpowered Hawk, breaking his back like a slender reed.

In his final breaths, Hawk spoke, “I sought not glory, but my father’s face, for my mother’s heart told me of him. Now I die without knowing him. Yet if the Great Spirit watches, my father shall find me and avenge my passing.”

Mountain’s sword fell from trembling hands; tears carved rivers down his face. Wracked with grief, he sought proof. Hawk showed him the black stone tied to his arm—and mountain and son were finally united in truth, though separated by fate.

Mountain wailed beneath the opening sky, mourning the strength and life lost to fate’s cruel hand. He wrapped Hawk in robes woven with golden thread and laid him to rest beneath a burial mound shaped like a great horse’s hoof, fragrant with cedar and sweetgrass. The village mourned long, their songs filled with sorrow and honor.

Far away, Morningstar, too, grieved. She tore her hair and cast ashes upon her head, carrying Hawk’s mantle and sword close to her heart. After a long year, her spirit joined her son, and the forest whispered their names forever.

Thus is the tale of Mountain and Hawk, a legend of courage and loss, reminding all that the ties of blood and spirit run deep and life’s greatest sorrow is parting from those we cherish most.
