Gulistan Chapter II- sotry 20

Long ago, beneath the vast skies where the Great Spirit breathes life into the earth, there lived a young man guided by the wisdom of a revered elder, known as the Keeper of Traditions. This elder, Abulfaraj, urged the youth to seek the quiet paths of solitude and reflection, avoiding the distractions of songs and celebrations. Yet, the fire of youth burned fiercely within his chest, awakening a longing to dance to the drumbeats and join the circle of voices beneath the stars.

Despite the elder’s counsel, the young man found himself drawn one night to a gathering by the firelight, where the people had come together to share stories and music. There, he watched a solitary musician, a man who drew the bow across his native flute with such intensity it seemed as though he sought to call the very heartbeat from the earth. But the sound he produced was not like the soft whispers of the wind through the pines—it was raw, jarring, like the cry of a lone wolf mourning a lost kin.

Some in the circle covered their ears, others pressed silent fingers to their lips, yet the music persisted. The singer’s voice was rough, not sweet like the songbirds of the meadow, but strange—making the listener restless and eager for peace. The young man wished to escape, pleading inwardly, "Let the morning come swiftly, or grant me ears that cannot hear this sorrow."

Still, he stayed through the long night, honoring his companions and the gathering’s spirit. The hoot of the owl, mistaken for the call to prayer, marked the passing of hours, yet sleep never found him under the watchful gaze of the stars.

At dawn, he took his feathered headband and a sacred shell, tokens of respect, and offered them to the musician with gratitude and embrace. His friends whispered and laughed, surprised by his praise for a man who, they said, had never held wealth nor comfort, wandering from place to place like the restless wind. They claimed the musician’s voice stirred fear and confusion, unsettling both mind and heart, and they doubted his worth.

The young man silenced their tongues, saying, "Speak no ill, for tonight I have heard a truth revealed in his song." When asked why he held such respect, he answered, "Though my elder counseled me to avoid such revelry, it was fate and the Great Spirit that guided me here. In this music, I found a lesson and a repentance—I will no longer walk blindly from the songs of our people."

For there is a power in a true voice, born from a pure heart and resonant soul, that captivates like the eagle’s flight or the whisper of the river. Yet, even the sweetest melodies of distant lands cannot soothe a heart when sung from a spirit that is not free.

Thus, the young man learned that night to honor the music of life in all its forms, and to listen beyond the surface—to the pulse beneath the song.
