The fox that rode a camel

Once there was a fox who lived near a garden. Every day, he would slip through a hole in the garden wall and feast on the delicious fruit growing there—juicy persimmons, sweet peaches, and ripe tangerines. After filling himself with fruit, he’d try to catch a chicken or two for dinner.

This went on until one morning when the gardener spotted the fox and began chasing him with a broomstick. The fox tried to escape through the usual hole, but found it sealed with fresh mud plaster. Trapped and seeing the gardener coming closer with a large stick, the fox decided to feign death. He dropped to the ground, rolled onto his back, and closed his eyes tightly.

The gardener reached the fox and, seeing him lying still, poked him with his stick, but the fox didn’t move. Thinking the fox had died of fright, the gardener picked him up and threw him out of the garden.

Days passed, and the fox found no way back into the garden. Starving, he devised a new plan. He decided to visit a lion he knew living in a nearby forest, hoping to scavenge some leftovers from the lion’s meal. After walking a long way, he arrived at the den—a cave near the forest—and called out, “Hello, lion! It’s your old friend, the fox, here to visit you!”

The lion came out and roared, “Hello, fox! What brings you here? How have you been?”

The fox replied sadly, “I traveled all this way just to see you, because what are friends for if not to visit each other? But don’t ask how I am—I carry many burdens and much sadness today.”

The lion asked gently, “What’s wrong, fox?”

“I haven’t eaten in days,” the fox said. “My belly is empty because I couldn’t find any food on the way here. Do you have anything to share?”

The lion replied, “I’m sorry, fox. I recently caught a fat boar, but I ate it all myself and have nothing left. If you can wait, I’ll hunt again tomorrow.”

But the fox was too hungry to wait. “No trouble,” he said. “I know a foolish camel who works at a grain mill nearby. Maybe I can trick him into coming here, so we both can eat!”

The lion agreed and said he’d wait for the camel.

The fox traveled to the neighboring village where the camel worked, turning the millstone day after day to grind wheat and oats into flour. When the fox approached, the camel greeted him, “Hello, fox! What brings you here?”

“I’ve come a long way to visit you, old friend,” said the fox happily. “What are friends for if not to visit one another?”

The camel asked how the fox was, and the fox said he was happier than ever because he was on his way to a secret land where the grass is always green, there’s plenty to eat, and no one has to work. Lowering his voice, the fox asked, “Why do you spend your days walking in circles? Is that how you want to waste your life?”

The camel said he needed to work to earn food and shelter provided by the miller and, though the work was hard, he was content.

The fox pretended to think deeply, flicking his tail, then exclaimed, “I have an idea! Come with me to my secret land. I want your company, and there you’ll never have to grind a millstone again!”

The camel thought it over and agreed. The fox bit through the camel’s yoke and freed him from the millstone. Then the fox said, “Since I’ve been walking for a long time, may I ride your back as you guide me to this secret land?”

The camel, easygoing, agreed, and the fox jumped on his back. As they traveled toward the forest, the fox whispered lies about the secret land to keep the camel distracted.

When they neared the lion’s den, the camel spotted the lion hiding in the bushes and realized it was a trap. “How can I walk myself to doom?” he thought. He turned around and started heading back to the mill.

The fox asked, “Why are you turning back, camel?”

“Oh no! I forgot something important and must fetch it immediately—I can’t go anywhere without it!” the camel said.

“What could be so important?” the fox demanded.

“A book of advice my father left me. I must sleep with it under my pillow every night or else I have bad dreams,” the camel said, quickening his pace to escape.

The fox, unable to stop him, suggested, “No worries. We’ll get your book, and come back tomorrow. But tell me, what advice did your father write down that’s so important?”

The camel, catching his breath, said, “There were five pieces of advice. The first: ‘Never go anywhere without this book.’ I forgot the others but will remember them once I’m back at the mill.”

Once safely back, the camel sighed and recited the rest: “Second, ‘There’s no shame in honest work.’ Third, ‘Be thankful for what you have.’ Fourth, ‘Never be friends with tricksters, for in the end they will trick you too.’ And the fifth, most important, ‘Don’t go near lions!’”

With that, the camel shook the fox off his back and returned to his millstone.
