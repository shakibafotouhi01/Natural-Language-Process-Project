THE FROG-PRINCE

One pleasant evening, a young princess put on her bonnet and wooden shoes and went for a walk alone in the forest. When she came to a cool spring of water in the middle of the woods, she sat down to rest for a while. She was holding her favorite toy, a golden ball, which she liked to toss into the air and catch again. After a while, she threw it up so high that she missed catching it, and the ball bounced away and rolled on the ground until it finally fell into the spring. The princess looked into the deep water but couldn’t see the bottom. She began to cry and said, “Oh no! If only I could get my ball back, I would give all my fine clothes, jewels, and everything I own.”

While she was speaking, a frog popped its head out of the water and asked, “Princess, why are you crying so badly?” She replied, “What can you do for me, you ugly frog? My golden ball fell into the spring.” The frog said, “I don’t want your jewels or fine clothes. But if you love me, let me live with you, eat from your golden plate, and sleep on your bed, I will get your ball back.” The princess thought to herself, “What nonsense! This silly frog can’t even leave the spring. But if he can get my ball, I’ll promise him whatever he wants.” So she said, “Alright, if you bring me my ball, I’ll do as you ask.” The frog then dived deep underwater and soon resurfaced with the ball in his mouth, tossing it onto the spring’s edge. The princess was so happy to have her ball again that she forgot all about the frog and ran home as fast as she could. The frog called after her, “Wait, princess, take me with you as you promised!” But she didn’t stop.

The next day, just as the princess sat down to dinner, she heard a strange noise—tap, tap—plash, plash—like something was climbing the marble stairs. Soon there was a gentle knock on the door and a small voice called:

“Open the door, my dear princess,  
Open it to your true love here!  
Remember the words that you and I said  
By the cool fountain in the green forest shade.”

The princess ran to the door and opened it, and there was the frog she had forgotten. Frightened, she quickly shut the door and went back to her seat. Her father, the king, asked what was wrong. “There’s a nasty frog at the door,” she said, “the one who got my ball out of the spring this morning. I told him he could live with me, thinking he couldn’t leave the spring—but now he’s at the door wanting to come in.”

As she spoke, the frog knocked again and said:

“Open the door, my dear princess,  
Open it to your true love here!  
Remember the words that you and I said  
By the cool fountain in the green forest shade.”

The king told his daughter, “Since you gave your word, you must keep it. Go and let him in.” Reluctantly, she did. The frog hopped inside and then hopped all around the room until he reached the table where the princess sat. “Please lift me onto a chair,” the frog said, “and let me sit beside you.” She did so, and he said, “Move your plate closer to me so I can eat.” She did, and when he had finished, he said, “Now I am tired. Carry me upstairs and put me to bed.” Though unwilling, the princess carried him to her bed, placed him on her pillow, and he slept all night. At dawn, he jumped up, hopped downstairs, and left the house. “Now he’s gone at last,” the princess thought, “and won’t trouble me anymore.”

But she was wrong. That very night, she heard the same tapping at the door. The frog came again and said:

“Open the door, my dear princess,  
Open it to your true love here!  
Remember the words that you and I said  
By the cool fountain in the green forest shade.”

When the princess opened the door, the frog came in and slept on her pillow as before until morning. The next two nights he did the same thing. But on the morning after the third night, she was amazed to find, instead of the frog, a handsome prince looking at her with the most beautiful eyes she had ever seen, standing at the head of her bed.

He told her that an evil fairy had enchanted him, turning him into a frog. He had to remain so until a princess rescued him from the spring, let him eat from her plate, and sleep in her bed for three nights. “You have broken this cruel spell,” he said, “and now I wish only to take you to my father’s kingdom, marry you, and love you forever.”

The young princess eagerly agreed. Just then, a splendid coach arrived, pulled by eight magnificent horses adorned with feather plumes and golden harnesses. Behind the coach rode the prince’s loyal servant, Heinrich, who had grieved bitterly over his master’s curse. They said farewell to the king, climbed into the coach, and with joy and celebration, journeyed to the prince’s kingdom. There, they lived happily for many years.
