THE GOLDEN GOOSE

There was a man with three sons. The youngest was called Dummling, and everyone looked down on him, mocked and sneered at him whenever they could.

One day, the eldest son wanted to go into the forest to chop wood. Before he left, his mother gave him a beautiful sweet cake and a bottle of wine so he wouldn’t go hungry or thirsty. In the forest, he met a little old grey-haired man who greeted him and asked if he could have a piece of the cake and a sip of wine because he was very hungry and thirsty. But the eldest son replied, “If I give you my cake and wine, I’ll have none left for myself. Get lost,” and left the old man standing there. Soon after, while chopping a tree, he swung his axe the wrong way and cut his arm. He had to go home and get it bandaged. This accident was the old man’s doing.

Next, the second son went into the forest. His mother gave him the same cake and bottle of wine. The little grey man met him as well and asked for some cake and wine, but the second son answered just as sensibly: “If I give you mine, I won’t have any for myself—go away!” and left him standing there. The second son struck his leg with the axe soon after and was so hurt he had to be carried home.

Then Dummling said, “Father, let me go cut wood.” His father replied, “Your brothers hurt themselves, leave it alone—you don’t know what you’re doing.” But Dummling begged so much that at last his father said, “Alright, go then; you might learn something by getting hurt.” His mother gave him a cake made with water and baked in ashes, along with a bottle of sour beer.

When Dummling reached the forest, the little old grey man greeted him and asked for some cake and a drink. Dummling said, “All I have is cinder-cake and sour beer—if you don’t mind, we’ll sit down and share it.” They sat, and when Dummling pulled out his cake, it was actually a fine sweet cake, and the sour beer had turned into good wine. After they ate and drank, the old man said, “Because you have a good heart and are willing to share, I’ll give you good luck. See that old tree? Cut it down, and you’ll find something at its roots.” Then he left.

Dummling cut down the tree, and when it fell, a goose with pure golden feathers was sitting among the roots. He picked her up and took her to an inn where he planned to spend the night. The innkeeper had three daughters who saw the golden goose and were curious, each wanting a golden feather.

The eldest daughter thought, “I’ll find a chance to pull out a feather,” and as soon as Dummling stepped outside, she grabbed the goose’s wing—but her hand stuck fast and wouldn’t let go.

Soon the second daughter came, hoping to get a feather, but when she touched her sister, she too got stuck fast.

Finally, the third daughter came. The others warned her to keep away, but she didn’t understand why and ran to them. When she touched her sister, she got stuck as well. So they had to spend the night attached to the goose.

The next morning, Dummling took the goose under his arm and set off, ignoring the three girls clinging to her. They had to follow wherever he went, left or right.

Out in the fields, a parson saw this odd procession and scolded the girls for running after the young man in such a way. When he grabbed the youngest girl's hand to pull her away, he got stuck too and had to follow along.

Not long after, the sexton passed by and, seeing his parson running with the three girls, called out and ran after him, grabbing his sleeve—and was held fast as well.

While the five of them ran one after the other, two laborers came from the fields with hoes. The parson begged them to free him and the sexton, but the moment they touched the sexton, they too got stuck. Now seven people were running behind Dummling and the golden goose.

Soon they reached a city ruled by a king whose daughter was so serious that no one could make her laugh. He had declared that anyone who made her laugh could marry her. When Dummling heard this, he went before the princess with the goose and all the people stuck to it. When she saw the odd sight of the seven people running one behind the other, she laughed out loud, as if she could never stop.

Dummling asked to marry her, but the king disapproved of him and made a difficult demand: Dummling must first produce a man who could drink an entire cellar of wine.

Dummling thought of the little grey man who had helped him before. He went back into the forest and found a man sitting sadly nearby. Dummling asked why he was so down, and the man explained, “I’m terribly thirsty and can’t quench it. I’ve just emptied a barrel of wine, but it was like a drop on a hot stone.”

“Come with me,” said Dummling. They went to the king’s cellar, and the man drank and drank until all the barrels were empty and his back hurt.

Then Dummling asked to marry the princess again. The king was angry, but set a new challenge: Dummling must find a man who could eat a whole mountain of bread.

Dummling returned to the forest, where he found a man tying a strap tight around his belly, making a painful face. “I’ve eaten an ovenful of rolls, but I’m still starving. I have to tie myself up or I’ll die from hunger.”

Dummling said, “Get up and come with me—you’ll be full soon.” He led him to the king’s palace, where a huge mountain of bread was baked from all the flour in the kingdom. The man ate until the entire mountain was gone by the end of the day.

Dummling asked again for the princess’s hand, but once more the king made a new demand: he must bring a ship that could sail on land and water. “When you return in it,” the king said, “you’ll have my daughter.”

Dummling went back to the forest, where the little grey man was waiting. When Dummling told him what he needed, the old man said, “Because you shared with me before, I’ll give you the ship.” He handed over the marvelous ship that could sail both on land and on water.

When the king saw this, he could no longer refuse Dummling’s marriage to his daughter. They celebrated the wedding, and after the king died, Dummling inherited the kingdom and lived happily for many years with his wife.
