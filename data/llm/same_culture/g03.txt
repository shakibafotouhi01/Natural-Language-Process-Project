RAPUNZEL

A man and woman longed for a child but had none. At last, the woman believed God might grant her wish. Their house had a small window at the back, looking out onto a wonderful garden full of beautiful flowers and herbs. But the garden was surrounded by a tall wall, and no one dared enter because it belonged to a powerful and feared enchantress. One day, the woman stood watching the garden and saw a bed of the freshest, greenest rampion (rapunzel). She grew sick with desire for it, becoming pale and weak. Her husband worried and asked what was wrong. She replied that if she couldn’t eat some rampion from the garden, she would surely die. Moved by love, the man decided to risk everything and climbed into the garden at twilight to gather some rampion. He brought it home, and his wife ate it eagerly. It tasted so good that the next day she longed for it even more. So the husband went back to the garden again at dusk, but this time the enchantress caught him. She demanded to know how he dared steal from her. He begged mercy, explaining his wife’s desperate craving. The enchantress softened and made a bargain: he could take as much rampion as he liked—but when the wife had a child, the enchantress would have the baby to raise herself. Terrified, the man agreed. When the child was born, the enchantress appeared, named the girl Rapunzel, and took her away.

Rapunzel grew into the most beautiful child under the sun. When she was twelve, the enchantress locked her in a tower in a forest, with no stairs or door—only a small window at the top. When the enchantress wanted to visit, she stood below the window and called out:

“Rapunzel, Rapunzel, let down your hair to me.”

Rapunzel had long, golden hair, finer than spun gold. Hearing the voice, she unbraided her hair, wrapped it around a hook, and let it hang down the tower. The enchantress climbed up by it.

After a year or two, the king’s son rode through the forest and heard Rapunzel singing. Her voice was so enchanting he stopped to listen. He tried to find a way up to her, but the tower had no door. Deeply moved, he came every day to hear her sing. One day he saw the enchantress arrive and heard her call:

“Rapunzel, Rapunzel, let down your hair to me.”

Rapunzel let her hair down, and the enchantress climbed up. The prince thought, “If that’s the way, I’ll try too.” The next evening, at dusk, he called up:

“Rapunzel, Rapunzel, let down your hair to me.”

Her hair fell, and the prince climbed up.

At first, Rapunzel was terrified to see a man for the first time. But the prince spoke kindly, telling her how deeply her singing had touched his heart. She grew less afraid and, when he asked her to be his wife, she saw he was young and handsome and agreed. She said, “I will go with you gladly, but I don’t know how to get down. Bring me a skein of silk each time you come. I will weave a ladder, and once it’s finished, I’ll climb down and you’ll take me away on your horse.” They agreed he would come nightly until then, as the enchantress came by day.

The enchantress noticed nothing at first, until one day Rapunzel said, “Tell me, Dame Gothel, why is it so much harder to pull you up than the young prince? He climbs quickly.” Enraged, the enchantress cried, “Wicked child! I thought I’d cut you off from the world, but you’ve deceived me!” She grabbed Rapunzel's golden hair, twisted it around her hand, and with a pair of scissors, cut off all her braids. Then she cast Rapunzel into a desolate wilderness, where the girl lived in sorrow and misery.

That same day, the enchantress nailed the cut hair to the window hook. When the prince arrived and called, “Rapunzel, Rapunzel, let down your hair to me,” she let the hair down. The prince climbed up, but instead of his beloved, he found the enchantress, who looked at him with fierce hatred. “Ah,” she sneered, “you came for your dear one, but the beautiful bird no longer sings in the nest. The cat has her now and will scratch out your eyes.” Heartbroken, the prince threw himself from the tower. He survived but fell onto thorn bushes, which blinded him. Blind and lost, he wandered the forest, eating roots and berries, mourning his lost love. After many years, he stumbled upon the wilderness where Rapunzel lived with their twin children, a boy and a girl. Hearing a familiar voice, he went toward it. Rapunzel recognized him, embraced him, and wept. Two of her tears fell on his eyes, and his sight returned. Overjoyed, he led Rapunzel and their children to his kingdom where they were welcomed warmly, and they lived happily ever after.

A forester went hunting in the forest when he heard a child crying. He followed the sound and found a little boy sitting in the top of a tall tree. The child’s mother had fallen asleep under the tree, holding him, when a bird of prey swooped down, snatched him, and placed him high in the branches. The forester climbed up and rescued the boy. He took him home to raise alongside his own daughter, Lina. The boy was called Fundevogel, meaning “found bird.” The two children grew up loving each other deeply and felt sad any time they were apart.

The forester had an old cook named Sanna, who one evening was seen carrying many buckets of water from the spring into the house, going back and forth many times. Curious, Lina asked why she was doing that. After promising not to tell, Sanna whispered that the next morning, while the forester was hunting, she planned to boil Fundevogel in a kettle of hot water.

Early next morning, the forester left for the hunt, and the children were still asleep. Lina said to Fundevogel, “If you never leave me, I’ll never leave you.” He promised never to leave. She then shared Sanna’s plan to kill him by boiling. They woke, dressed quickly, and ran away together.

When Sanna came to fetch Fundevogel, both children were gone. Alarmed, she sent three servants to find and bring them back. The children, watching from afar, saw the servants approach. Lina told Fundevogel to never leave her, and they transformed: he became a rose tree, and she a rose on it. The servants reached the place but found only a rose tree with one rose. They returned and told Sanna. She scolded them for not cutting the tree and rose to bring home, and sent them out again.

The children transformed again: Fundevogel became a church, Lina the chandelier inside. The servants found nothing but the church and chandelier, then returned. Sanna scolded them for not tearing down the church and taking the chandelier, and decided to go herself.

When she approached, Lina told Fundevogel to never leave her, and they changed once more: he became a fishpond, she a duck floating on it. Sanna came close, lay down to drink, but the duck quickly grabbed her head, pulling her under the water to drown. Freed from the witch’s threat, the children went home together, happy and safe. And if they are not dead, they live happily still.
