The Man with his Leg Tied Up

Long ago, Aggo Dah Gauda was punished for once using his foot against a respected medicine man. One of his legs was tied up to his thigh, forcing him to hop wherever he went. With practice, he became very skilled at hopping and could leap with amazing agility.

Aggo had a beautiful daughter. His greatest concern was to protect her from being taken by the king of the buffalos, the powerful ruler of all the buffalo herds who commanded them completely. Dah Gauda himself was important in his own right, living in a grand log house with a courtyard stretching as far west as he wished to claim. Despite his vast domain, he warned his daughter to stay inside the lodge, never wandering far, because he was certain the buffalo-king was always lurking nearby, waiting to seize her.

One bright morning, with a few gentle clouds drifting across the sky, Aggo prepared to go fishing. Before leaving, he reminded his daughter about the dangerous stranger she had never seen.

“My daughter,” he said, “I’m going out to fish. The day will be fine, but remember our enemy is near, watching with eyes that never close. Do not show yourself outside the lodge.”

With that advice, Aggo hopped away happily. Soon after reaching the fishing spot, he heard a strange song carried on the wind:

Man with the leg tied up,  
Man with the leg tied up,  
Broken hip—hip—  
Hipped.  
Man with the leg tied up,  
Man with the leg tied up,  
Broken leg—leg—  
Legged.

No one was visible, but Aggo clearly heard the words. Knowing this must be the work of his enemies, the buffalos, he hurried home as fast as his one leg could carry him.

Meanwhile, left alone inside the lodge, the daughter thought to herself, “It’s so hard always staying inside. But father says it’s dangerous to go out. I know what I’ll do—I’ll climb to the top of the house. There I can comb my hair, and no one can harm me.”

She climbed to the roof and began untying and combing her long, beautiful hair. Her hair was truly remarkable—not only shiny and fine but so long that it draped over the eaves and touched the ground as she sat there grooming it. She was so focused on this that she didn’t notice the buffalo-king rushing in with his herd. He grabbed her by her cascading hair, placed her on the back of one of his chosen buffaloes, and galloped across the plains. Splashing through a river at the edge of his land, he carried her safely to his lodge on the far shore.

Now that he had Aggo Dah Gauda’s daughter, the buffalo-king set to win her heart. It might have been smarter to try this before taking her, but now his efforts were mostly in vain. Although he spoke kindly to her and treated her with great care, she sat silent and sorrowful among the other females, showing no interest in the king or his household. To him, she was as still and quiet as a bush by her father’s door when the summer wind has died away.

The king commanded everyone in the lodge, under threat of death, to give her everything she wanted and never upset her. They brought her the finest food and gave her the place of honor. The king himself went hunting for the best meats and birds to please her. Each morning he took her on a gentle ride on a royal buffalo, whose calm gait didn’t disturb a single strand of her magnificent hair.

At times, the king would fast to clear his spirit and warm his voice. Then sitting before the lodge with an Indian flute, he poured out his feelings in lonely song:

My sweetheart,  
My sweetheart,  
Ah me!  
When I think of you,  
When I think of you,  
Ah me!  
What can I do, do, do?  
How I love you,  
How I love you,  
Ah me!  
Do not hate me,  
Do not hate me,  
Ah me!  
Speak—even scold me.  
When I think of you,  
Ah me!  
What can I do, do, do?

Meanwhile, Aggo Dah Gauda returned home to find his daughter missing. His anger flared fiercely. He wanted to tear out his hair, but being bald that was impossible. So, to vent, he hopped off half a mile in every direction—east, west, north, and south—searching for her until his one leg tired out. Then he rested in his lodge and vowed his single leg would never rest again until he found and brought her home. With determination, he set out.

Going more slowly now, Aggo tracked the buffalo-king to the riverbank where wild buffalo had crossed. But after a few cold nights, the water was frozen with thin ice too fragile for him. He camped nearby until the ice was strong enough to cross and then followed the trail.

Along the way, he found broken branches scattered behind him. These were signs his daughter left, for, when caught, her loose long hair had caught on branches, and she had snapped off twigs to mark the path.

Finally, Aggo came upon the buffalo-king’s lodge at evening. Peeking inside, he saw his daughter sitting silently. When their eyes met, she recognized him and, her heart softening, she asked for the royal dipper, saying, “I will go get the king some water.”

The buffalo-king, hopeful at this sign of submission, awaited her return eagerly.

But she slipped away without a sound. The king called his herd and they set off across the plains. Under the moonlight, they spotted Aggo Dah Gauda, who carried his daughter in his arms, hopping swiftly to the west.

The buffalos gave a loud shout and chased after him, sure they would catch him quickly. But although Aggo had only one leg, it was so strong and swift that for every step they took, he hopped the length of a cedar tree.

The buffalo-king was determined not to be outrun by a man with a tied-up leg. Shouting and urging, he sent his fastest buffaloes ahead with strict orders to capture Aggo dead or alive. It was a strange sight: when one buffalo nearly reached him, Aggo would leap far off to the side, putting himself out of reach. This brought him close to another buffalo, then he’d hop away again, zigzagging across the plain.

The buffalos grew confused and frantic. They roared, flicked their tails, and foamed, as if trying to scare the old man in the moon watching from above the prairie.

Finally, the frustrated king lost patience. Chasing a one-legged man all night seemed ridiculous, so he called off the hunt and fled west, never to return to that land.

Free from pursuit, Aggo hopped swiftly across a stream and brought his daughter safely home.

Later, Aggo’s beautiful daughter married a fine young warrior who was no buffalo-king and owned nothing but a splendid buffalo skin robe, worn proudly over his shoulders on their wedding day. On that day, Aggo Dah Gauda hopped about on his one leg more lively than ever before.
