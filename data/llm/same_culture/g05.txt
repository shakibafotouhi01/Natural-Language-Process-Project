RUMPELSTILTSKIN

Near a forest in a distant land, a clear stream flowed beside a mill, and nearby stood the miller’s house. The miller had a very beautiful daughter, who was also clever and sharp. Proud of her, the miller told the king, who often hunted in the woods, that his daughter could spin straw into gold. The king, who loved money, became greedy and ordered the girl to be brought before him. He took her to a room filled with straw, gave her a spinning wheel, and commanded, “Spin all this straw into gold by morning, or your life is forfeit.” The poor girl protested that this was only her father’s boast and she could not do such a thing, but the door was locked, and she was left alone.

Sitting down in a corner, she cried for her hard fate. Suddenly, the door opened, and a strange little man hobbled in. “Good morning, young lady. Why are you crying?” he asked. She told him of her impossible task. “What will you give me to spin it into gold for you?” he asked. She offered her necklace, and he agreed. Sitting at the wheel, he whistled and sang:

“Round about, round about,  
Look and see!  
Reel and spin,  
Straw to gold!”

The wheel turned quickly, and by morning all the straw was spun into gold.

When the king saw the gold, he was amazed and pleased, but his greed grew stronger. He locked the miller’s daughter in a bigger room filled with more straw and ordered her to spin it all into gold by morning. Again, she wept not knowing what to do. The little man appeared and asked, “What will you give me now?” She offered her ring. He took it and began to spin, singing his song, until all the straw was gold before dawn.

The king was overjoyed but still desired more. He led the miller’s daughter to an even larger pile of straw and declared that if she spun it all by morning, she could become his queen. Alone once more, the dwarf appeared and asked what she would give him this time. She had nothing left but agreed to give him the first child she would bear as queen. The little man agreed, spun the straw to gold again, and the king, seeing the gold, married her, making her queen.

When her first child was born, the queen was happy and forgot the dwarf and their promise. One day, the little man appeared in her room while she played with her baby and reminded her of the bargain. She was heartbroken and offered him all her kingdom’s wealth to let her keep the child, but he refused. Finally, moved by her tears, he said, “I will give you three days. If by then you can guess my name, you may keep your baby.”

The queen stayed awake all night thinking of every name she knew and sent messengers far and wide to find unusual names. On the first day, when the dwarf returned, she guessed many names—Timothy, Ichabod, Benjamin, Jeremiah—but he denied each one.

On the second day, she tried funny names—Bandy-legs, Hunchback, Crook-shanks—but he rejected those too.

On the third day, a messenger returned with news. While climbing a hill through the forest where fox and hare said goodnight, he had spotted a small hut. By the fire stood a funny little dwarf dancing on one leg, singing:

“Merrily I feast and bake,  
Today I’ll brew and make;  
Next day a stranger I will bring—  
Little does my lady guess my name:  
Rumpelstiltskin!”

Hearing this, the queen was joyful. When the little man came again, she gathered her court around her throne, the nurse beside her holding the baby as if ready to give him up. The dwarf grinned, certain the child was his. “Tell me my name,” he demanded. “Is it John?” she guessed. “No,” he said. “Tom?” “No.” “Jimmy?” “No.” “Is it Rumpelstiltskin?” she asked slyly. The little man shouted, “A witch told you that! A witch told you that!” In a furious rage, he stamped his right foot so hard into the floor that he had to grab it with both hands to pull it out.

Then he ran off as fast as he could. The nurse laughed, the baby cooed, and the whole court jeered at him for all his trouble, crying out, “Good morning, Mr. Rumpelstiltskin, and happy feasting!”
