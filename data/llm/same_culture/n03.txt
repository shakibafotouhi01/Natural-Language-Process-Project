Legend of the Corn

The Arikara were the first to discover maize. One day, a young man went out hunting and climbed a high hill. Looking down into the valley, he saw a buffalo bull standing near where two rivers met. He planned to kill the buffalo but first took in the beauty of the land—the rivers’ low banks lined with many trees. The buffalo faced north, so he couldn’t get close enough with his bow. He decided to wait until the buffalo moved closer to the riverbanks or to a ravine full of bushes.

The sun set, but the buffalo did not move.

That night the hunter stayed awake, hungry and frustrated. Before dawn, he hurried back to the hilltop. The buffalo stood still, now facing east. He waited all day again, the sun going down with the buffalo still in the same spot.

That night he barely ate and did not sleep well. Early the next morning, as the sun rose, he checked the buffalo. It remained standing exactly where it was, now facing south. He waited through the day once more, and when the sun set, the buffalo had not moved.

On the fourth morning, the hunter rushed up the hill at sunrise. The buffalo still stood there, now facing west. He watched all day long, but the buffalo did not budge.

He wondered, “Why won’t the buffalo move?” It didn’t drink, eat, or sleep. He sensed some mighty power was at work.

The following morning, he hurried to the hilltop as soon as dawn brightened the land. But the buffalo was gone. In its place was a strange bush.

He approached carefully and realized it was a new kind of plant. Tracing the buffalo’s footprints, he saw where it had turned east, south, and west. In the center of these paths was a single track, and from that spot, the small plant had grown. There were no tracks showing the buffalo’s departure.

Excited, the hunter ran back to his village and shared his story with the chiefs and people about the mysterious buffalo and the new plant. Curious, all the chiefs and people came to see the place. They looked closely at the buffalo tracks, noting it had stood in the same spot but had left without leaving a path behind.

The people understood then that Wahkoda, the Great Spirit, had given them this strange plant. They already knew many plants they could eat and when each ripened. So, they guarded this new plant carefully.

A flower soon blossomed on it. Later, a new shoot appeared at one of its joints, covered in fine hair that started green and turned brown. The people thought the fruit might be ripe but were afraid to touch it. They gathered to talk over what they should do.

A young man spoke up, “My life has not been good. If any harm comes to me, I do not care.”

Encouraged by him, the people agreed. The young man reached out, placing his hand on the plant and then on the fruit. He grasped it firmly and declared, “It is solid. It is ripe.” He peeled back the husks and said, “It is red.”

He took some of the grains, showed them to the people, and ate several. He did not die. Now everyone knew Wahkoda had sent this plant to nourish them.

When fall came and the prairie grasses turned brown, the plant’s leaves browned as well. The fruit was harvested and stored. After winter passed, the kernels were divided, four per family.

The people moved their lodges closer to where the plant had grown. When the hills turned green again, they planted those seeds, first building small mounds like the one where the first plant had grown. The corn grew tall and ripe, its ears bright with many colors—red, yellow, white, and blue.

The next year, many plants and many ears of corn grew. The Arikara invited other tribes to visit and shared this new food with them. That is how the Omahas first came to have corn.
