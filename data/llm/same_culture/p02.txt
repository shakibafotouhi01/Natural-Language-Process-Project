Gulistan - story 16

A friend of mine once lamented about the hard times he was living through. He told me he had a small income and a large family, without the strength to handle the burden of poverty. Often, he thought about moving to another country so that, no matter how he lived, no one would know whether his fortune was good or bad.

He said many people sleep hungry, and no one even knows who they are. Many lie near death, but no one mourns for them.

He was also worried about his enemies, who would mock him behind his back, accusing him of struggling for his family out of weakness rather than strength, and saying:

“Look at that dishonorable man who will never 
Know prosperity’s face,
He chooses comfort for himself,
Leaving his wife and children to suffer.”

He also asked me, since I knew he had some skill with numbers, if I could use my influence to get him a position. This would ease his mind and make him indebted to me, a debt he could never repay with simple thanks. I answered, “My friend, jobs under a king come with two things: hope for bread and risk to life. Wise men say it's unwise to take such risks just for hope.”

No one visits a dervish’s home
To demand taxes on his fields and gardens.
You must either accept your worries and sorrows
Or leave your beloved children to the crows.

He answered, “You have not really addressed my situation or answered my question. Have you not heard the saying, ‘Whoever betrays shall tremble when judged’?”

Honesty is the way to God’s favor.
I have never seen anyone lost who walked the straight path.

The wise say, “Four types of people live in fear of four others: a robber fears the sultan, a thief fears the watchman, an adulterer fears the informer, and a harlot fears the overseer. But who fears when their conscience is clear?”

Do not be wasteful while in power, if you want
Your enemies to be embarrassed when you leave.
Be pure, brother, and fear no one.
Washermen only beat dirty clothes on stones.

I said, “Your story reminds me of a fox I heard about. It was running with great fear and when asked why, it said, ‘I heard camels are being forced into service.’ People said, ‘Fool, what do you have to do with camels? You don’t resemble one.’ The fox replied, ‘Quiet! If my enemies maliciously call me a camel and I get caught, who will care to help or defend me? By the time a cure comes from Iraq, the person bitten by the snake dies.’ You are an excellent, honest man, but enemies lie in wait everywhere. If they twist your character, you’ll be forced to explain yourself to the king and face blame. Who dares speak on your behalf then? So, I think you should retreat into contentment and give up ambitions for power. The wise have said:”

“There are many treasures in the sea,
But if you want safety, stay on shore.”

My friend grew angry when he heard this. He frowned and scolded me, saying, “What kind of wisdom is that? The philosophers’ words prove true—friends are useful only in prison, for when dining, all enemies act like friends.”

Do not call a friend one who knocks on your door
Only when you are prosperous,
Boasting of friendship and calling himself your brother.
I call a friend one who takes the hand of a friend
When he is poor and distressed.

Seeing he had changed and accused me of speaking out of self-interest, I went to the President of the State Council. Trusting my old friendship with him, I explained my friend’s situation, and he gave my friend a small position. Soon my friend’s good nature and management earned praise, and he was promoted to a higher office. His fortune rose steadily until he reached the peak of his hopes, becoming a courtier to the sultan, well-respected and trusted. I felt happy for his stable position and said:

“Do not fear complicated troubles or carry a broken heart,
For life’s spring hides in darkness.”

Do not grieve, brother in hardship,
For the Merciless hides favor in shadows.

Do not sit gloomy over life’s changes;
Though patience is bitter, its fruit is sweet.

Later, I went on a pilgrimage to Mecca with friends, and on the way back, my friend met me two stages away, looking troubled and dressed like a dervish. I asked, “What happened?” He said, “As you predicted, some envied me and accused me of treason. The king did not even check if it was true. My old supporters, kind friends who should have spoken the truth, forgot all our past bonds.

“Don’t you see how before a man in power,
People bow and praise him,
But when fortune turns,
They all trample on his head?

“For weeks, I suffered persecution, until news arrived of pilgrims returning from Mecca. That day, I was freed from my heavy chains, but my inherited property was taken.” I replied, “You didn’t listen when I said serving kings is like sailing the sea—profitable but dangerous. You either gain treasure or are lost in the waves.”

The official either grabs gold with both hands on shore,
Or the waves one day throw him dead onto the shore.

Not wanting to increase my friend’s pain or rub salt into his wounds, I simply recited these two lines:

“Did you not know your feet would be in chains
If you cannot hear the advice of others?

And if you cannot stand the sting’s pain,
Do not put your finger inside a scorpion’s hole.”
