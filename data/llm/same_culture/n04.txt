The Creation Story – Turtle Island

Long ago, after Kitchi-Manitou, the Great Mystery, created the earth and the Anishinabe, or Original People, lived in harmony, things changed. The Anishinabe began to fight among themselves, turning brother against brother over hunting grounds and other disputes. Seeing that peace and respect had vanished from the world, Kitchi-Manitou decided to cleanse the earth with water.

A great flood, called mush-ko'-be-wun', covered the land, wiping out most of the Anishinabe and many animals. Only Nanaboozhoo, a central figure in many Anishinabe stories, survived, along with a few animals and birds that could swim or fly. Nanaboozhoo floated on a large log, searching for land, but none could be found under the endless floodwaters. He let the animals and birds rest on the log, taking turns.

Then Nanaboozhoo said, “I’m going to try something. I’ll dive to the bottom of this water and bring up some earth. With that small bit of earth and the help of the Four Winds and Kitchi-Manitou, we can create new land to live on.” He dove in and stayed underwater a long time but came up breathless, telling the animals the water was too deep to reach the bottom.

After a silence, Mahng the Loon spoke up. “I can dive deep; that’s how I catch my food. I’ll try.” The Loon disappeared underwater and stayed gone for a very long time. The others feared he had drowned, but finally, he surfaced, weak and nearly unconscious. “I couldn’t reach the bottom,” he gasped. “Maybe there is no bottom.”

Next, Zhing-gi-biss, the helldiver, said, “I’ll try,” and went down. A long time passed before he returned, unconscious. When he awoke, he said he also couldn't find the earth at the bottom.

Many animals tried after that: Zhon-gwayzh’, the mink, and even Mizhee-kay, the turtle. All failed. It seemed impossible to bring up earth from the deep water.

Then a quiet voice said, “I can do it.” No one saw who spoke at first, but then little Wazhushk, the muskrat, stepped forward. “I will try,” he said. Some larger animals laughed at him, but Nanaboozhoo said, “Only Kitchi-Manitou can judge. If muskrat wants to try, let him.”

Muskrat dove into the water and stayed underwater much longer than anyone else. The others feared he had died. Far below, muskrat reached the bottom. Weak and nearly out of air, he grabbed a small clump of earth in his paw and struggled back to the surface. One of the animals saw him floating up. Nanaboozhoo pulled him onto the log and said, “Friends, muskrat stayed under too long. He is dead.” The animals sang a mourning song as muskrat’s spirit passed on.

Suddenly, Nanaboozhoo exclaimed, “Look! There is something in his paw!” Carefully, he opened muskrat’s tiny paw. All the animals gathered close and saw a small ball of earth held tightly there. They shouted with joy. Muskrat had given his life to bring the earth to the surface so life could start again.

Nanaboozhoo took the earth from muskrat’s paw. The turtle swam forward and said, “Place the earth on my back. I will carry it. With Kitchi-Manitou’s help, we can create a new earth.” Nanaboozhoo set the earth on the turtle’s shell. Then the wind blew from each of the Four Directions, and the small earth began to grow. It grew and grew until it became a large island in the water.

As the island expanded, the turtle carried its weight patiently. Nanaboozhoo and the animals sang and danced around the growing land. When the winds finally stopped and the waters calmed, a great island rested in the midst of the water—this island is now called North America.

To this day, the Ojibway and other native peoples hold the turtle in great respect for carrying the earth and making life possible for the world's second people. The muskrat is also honored. Though marshes have been drained and homes destroyed, the muskrat survives and thrives, building its homes shaped like the little ball of earth and the island that grew from it, keeping alive the memory of the great flood and the new beginning it brought.
