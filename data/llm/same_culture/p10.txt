9  - Saiawosh

One day, Toos, Gew, Goodarz, and other mighty knights of Iran went into the forests of Daghoui to hunt wild asses. As they ventured deeper, they came across a woman of extraordinary beauty. Toos and Gew both fell deeply in love with her at once. When they asked about her family, they learned she was a descendant of Feridoun. Each man wished to marry her, but neither would yield to the other, and harsh words almost turned into a fight. Then someone suggested they let Kai Kawous decide. Agreeing, they took the fair woman before Kai Kawous and told him everything that had happened. But Kai Kawous, captivated by her beauty, desired her for himself, saying she was fit to sit on a throne. He took her and brought her into the palace of his women.

After some time, she gave birth to a son, a tall and strong boy named Saiawosh. Kai Kawous was joyful for his son, but he also worried, for the stars had foretold a troubling fate: the heavens were against the boy, and his virtues would not save him but rather lead to his destruction.

Meanwhile, the news of the Shah’s son reached Rostam’s land. Learning this, Rostam was lifted from his grief over Sohrab and traveled from Zaboulistan to ask for the child to raise him for Iran. Kai Kawous agreed, and Rostam took the boy back to his kingdom, training him in war and celebration. Saiawosh grew strong and handsome, unmatched in the world.

When Saiawosh was grown enough to trap a lion, he came before Rostam proudly and said he wished to meet the Shah, to show his father what kind of man Rostam had made of him. Rostam approved and led a great army toward Iran, with Saiawosh at its head. The land rejoiced to see Saiawosh, and the King’s courts celebrated joyously, showering Rostam and Saiawosh with treasures. Kai Kawous was delighted and placed Saiawosh beside him on the throne. All sang his praises, and a feast was held unlike any before.

Saiawosh stayed at his father’s court for seven years, proving his valor. In the eighth year, Kai Kawous crowned him and gave him a throne. All seemed well, and many forgot the dire prophecy from the stars. But fate was not so easily changed.

Sudaveh, seeing Saiawosh’s youth and beauty, fell deeply in love with him. She sent a message inviting him to the women’s quarters, but he refused, mistrusting her. Angered, Sudaveh complained to Kai Kawous that Saiawosh ignored her, and she persuaded the King to order the boy to spend time among his sisters. Saiawosh obeyed.

Once inside the women’s quarters, Sudaveh wanted to speak to him alone, but Saiawosh resisted. She tried three times, but he remained firm. Furious, Sudaveh slandered Saiawosh’s reputation and spread false stories about him. She turned Kai Kawous’s heart against his son. The King, torn between love for Sudaveh and his son, doubted who was truthful.

To test him, Kai Kawous built a massive pyre from logs so large it could be seen from two farsangs away, filling the air with smoke and fire when ignited. He commanded Saiawosh to ride through the flames to prove his innocence. Saiawosh prayed to God, charged his horse, and galloped into the fire unscathed, with not a hair singed.

The people rejoiced, but Sudaveh, watching from her roof, prayed for his destruction. Kai Kawous saw with his own eyes that Saiawosh was innocent and took him back to the throne, asking forgiveness for doubting him. They celebrated with three days of feasting and joy. Yet Sudaveh’s hold over the King grew stronger, and she filled his mind with worries about Saiawosh.

While Kai Kawous was distracted, Afrasiyab prepared an attack with three thousand warriors against Iran. The Shah was angered but resolved to lead his army himself. A Mubid warned him not to go, recalling past dangers, but Kai Kawous dismissed the advice, confident in his leadership.

Saiawosh saw this as his chance to prove himself. He asked his father to lead the army, reminding him that as his son of noble lineage, he had the right. Kai Kawous agreed, and messengers summoned Rostam to accompany Saiawosh as his protector. Rostam promised to guard him faithfully, proud to serve.

The army marched, trumpet sounds filling the air, with Saiawosh at the head, and Kai Kawous blessing them. They rested in Zaboulistan with Zal, gathering allies from kings near and far. After a month, they moved on to Balkh, where Afrasiyab’s forces, led by Gersiwaz, faced them.

Battle raged fiercely for three days until Iran claimed victory on the fourth. Saiawosh sent a musk-scented letter to his father, reporting the triumph. Kai Kawous rejoiced and replied with hopeful words.

Afrasiyab was crushed by the defeat and troubled by Gersiwaz’s report of Saiawosh’s power. That night, Afrasiyab dreamt of the earth filled with serpents and Iranians attacking him, which left him shaken and fearful. The Mubids told him the stars foretold Saiawosh’s victory over Turan, even if Afrasiyab himself killed him. They advised seeking peace rather than resisting destiny.

Afrasiyab agreed and sent Gersiwaz with rich gifts and a message proposing alliance and peace. Saiawosh and Rostam, suspicious, entertained Gersiwaz for seven days of feasting while weighing their options.

On the eighth day, Saiawosh agreed to peace but requested a hundred noble hostages from Turan as guarantee. Afrasiyab reluctantly sent the hostages and withdrew his army, returning conquered lands to Iran.

Rostam informed Kai Kawous of these events, praising Saiawosh’s wisdom in seeking peace. But Kai Kawous was outraged, deeming peace with Afrasiyab a childish mistake. He ordered Rostam to command Saiawosh to destroy the hostages and renew the attack.

Rostam refused, defending Saiawosh’s honorable conduct. Kai Kawous condemned Rostam’s counsel and sent him back to Seistan. He sent Toos to the army’s borders with his orders.

Saiawosh was troubled, torn between loyalty to his oath and the King’s command. He confided in Bahram and Zengueh, deciding to keep his promise and seek refuge from his father’s wrath. He sent Zengueh to Afrasiyab with a letter explaining the discord and his desire to hide where his name would be unknown to Kai Kawous.

Afrasyiab welcomed Zengueh warmly and, guided by Piran’s counsel, decided to receive Saiawosh as his son-in-law and ally, hoping that when Kai Kawous died, Saiawosh would end their feud and unite their lands in peace.

Afrasiyab sent a letter to Saiawosh affirming his welcome and trust. Saiawosh was glad but troubled to ally with his land’s enemy. He wrote to his father, explaining his plight, and entrusted Bahram to lead the army until Toos arrived.

Toos came, confused by events, and Kai Kawous was dismayed, cursing Afrasiyab and Saiawosh but holding back from war.

Saiawosh arrived in Turan to great honors. Piran greeted him with white elephants and gifts, assuring him of Afrasiyab’s goodwill despite his reputation. At Afrasiyab’s court, Saiawosh was embraced like a son; they feasted and played games, and Saiawosh’s strength and spirit delighted the King.

For twelve moons Saiawosh lived at Afrasiyab’s side. Then, following Piran’s advice, he asked for the hand of Afrasiyab’s daughter, Ferangis, who was given to him joyfully. A magnificent wedding followed, with gifts and a kingdom bestowed upon him. After a year of ruling his province, Saiawosh built the city of Gangdis, a place of unmatched beauty, planting countless trees and creating spaces for sport and joy.

Though the Mubids warned Gangdis would bring him misfortune, Saiawosh dismissed their words and reveled in his life and trust in Afrasiyab.

Yet the stars held their decree. Gersiwaz, jealous of Saiawosh’s favor and power, planned his ruin. With Afrasiyab’s permission, Gersiwaz visited Gangdis under friendly pretense, feasting and admiring Saiawosh’s wealth, all the while plotting against him.

Returning to Afrasiyab, Gersiwaz spread rumors that Saiawosh was conspiring with his father to destroy Turan. Afrasiyab, troubled but unwilling to believe, grew suspicious. Gersiwaz pressed him to act, and Afrasiyab fell into his trap.

He ordered Gersiwaz to summon Saiawosh to court to bring Ferangis for a feast. Saiawosh agreed, tying his horse’s bridle to the charger, ready to go. But Gersiwaz, fearing Saiawosh’s open nature would expose his lies, feigned sorrow and warned Saiawosh not to go, promising to soften Afrasiyab’s heart first.

Naively, Saiawosh agreed to stay; he sent greetings and excuses, claiming Ferangis was sick. Gersiwaz carried the letter but did not deliver it to Afrasiyab, instead poisoning him further with lies and urging war.

When the Turan army approached Gangdis, Gersiwaz warned Saiawosh to flee. Saiawosh, overcome, gave Ferangis instructions on what to do if he fell, refusing to fight against one he once called a father. He prepared for death.

At battle, Afrasiyab ignored Saiawosh’s attempts at reconciliation as Gersiwaz shouted accusations. Afrasiyab’s heart hardened against the beloved youth.

Saiawosh ordered his men not to fight back, and they were cut down around him. A knight seized him but did not kill him, binding him and bringing him before Afrasiyab, who ordered his execution in a barren place.

The nobles mourned, and before the execution, Ferangis demanded to see her father. Though Afrasiyab refused and imprisoned her, she pleaded for Saiawosh, warning that killing him would doom Turan and bring revenge from the Kaianides.

Afrasiyab, blinded by rage, cast her into a dungeon. Gersiwaz, sensing his chance, signaled the guards to kill Saiawosh. They dragged the young king into the desert and struck him down. Suddenly, a fierce storm darkened the skies. The executioners trembled, regretting their deed.

Ferangis’s cries reached Afrasiyab, who ordered her killed too. But Piran intervened, pleading for her life and offering to protect her as a daughter in his home. Afrasiyab agreed, glad to be rid of the reminder of his lost friend.

Piran took Ferangis beyond the mountains, while Afrasiyab returned sorrowful and restless, haunted by thoughts of Saiawosh and filled with regret over what he had done.
