THE RAVEN

Once there was a queen who had a little daughter, too young to walk on her own. One day, the child was very difficult to calm, no matter what her mother did. Growing impatient, the queen looked out the window and saw the ravens flying around the castle. She said, “I wish you were a raven and would just fly away so I could have some peace.” No sooner had she spoken than the child she held was turned into a raven and flew out the window. The bird flew to a dark forest and stayed there a long time, while the parents heard nothing from their daughter.

Long afterward, a man was walking through the forest when he heard a raven’s call and followed the sound. As he got closer, the raven said, “I was born a king’s daughter, but now I am under a curse. You can free me.” “What must I do?” the man asked. She told him, “Go deeper into the forest until you find a house where an old woman lives. She will offer you food and drink, but you must not accept either, or you will fall into a deep sleep and won’t be able to help me. Behind the house is a big tan heap, and you must stand there watching for me. For three days in a row, I will come in my carriage at two o’clock in the afternoon. The first day, it will be drawn by four white horses; the second day by four chestnut horses; and the third by four black horses. But if you fall asleep any day, I won’t be freed.”

The man promised to do as she said, but the raven sighed, “I fear even now that you will take something from the woman and fail to save me.” He once again promised not to eat or drink anything.

When the man entered the house, the old woman greeted him, saying, “You look so tired! Come and rest; let me give you some food and drink.” “No,” he replied, “I won’t eat or drink.” But she kept urging him, “If you won’t eat, at least have a sip of wine; one drink won’t matter.” Eventually, he gave in and drank.

As the appointed hour approached, he went out to the garden and climbed the tan heap to watch for the raven. Suddenly, he felt very tired and, despite his determination to stay awake, lay down. Within moments his eyes closed and he fell into a deep sleep no noise could break. When the raven’s carriage arrived, drawn by four white horses, she sighed, “I know he has fallen asleep.” She found him sleeping on the tan heap, called to and shook him, but in vain.

The next day, at noon, the old woman offered him food and drink again, which he again refused at first. Yet tired by her persistence, he eventually took another drink. At two o’clock, he went to the tan heap to watch for the raven but again felt overwhelmingly tired and fell asleep. The raven arrived with her four chestnut horses and sadly admitted, “I know he’s fallen asleep,” then found him asleep and could not wake him.

On the third day, the old woman asked, “Why won’t you eat or drink? Are you trying to harm yourself?” He replied firmly that he would neither eat nor drink. But when she set down food and wine again, though he tried to resist, he smelled the wine and couldn’t help drinking deeply.

When the time came, he once more went to the tan heap, but he felt even more exhausted than before and fell into an even deeper sleep. This time the raven’s carriage was drawn by black horses. She was more sorrowful than ever as she said, “I know he has fallen asleep and won’t be able to free me.” She found him deeply asleep, and nothing she tried could wake him. Then she placed beside him a loaf, some meat, and a flask of wine that never grew any less, no matter how much he ate or drank. She took a gold ring from her finger, engraved with her name, and put it on his finger. Finally, she laid a letter near him, explaining what she had left and writing, “I see you won’t free me as long as you stay here. But if you still want to help me, come to the golden castle of Stromberg; it’s well within your power.” Then she returned to her carriage and drove to the golden castle of Stromberg.

When the man woke and realized how long he'd slept, he felt sad. “She must have been here and left. It’s too late now to save her.” His eyes landed on the loaf, meat, wine, ring, and letter. Reading the letter, he understood everything. He got up immediately, eager to set out for Stromberg, though he didn’t know which way to go. He traveled for a long time, finally reaching a dark forest. He walked through it for fourteen days but couldn’t find a way out. One night, exhausted, he slept under a bush. The next day, while looking for a place to rest, he heard howling and crying that kept him awake. When it grew darker and people began lighting their homes, he saw a faint light ahead and went toward it.

He found a house that looked smaller than it really was compared to a huge giant standing in front of it. He thought, “If the giant sees me entering, my life might be at risk.” Still, he gathered courage and went on. The giant shouted, “Lucky you came! I’m starving and could make you my supper.” “I’d rather you didn’t,” said the man. “I won’t just give myself up. But if you’re hungry, I have enough food and drink to satisfy you.” “If that’s true,” the giant said, “I’ll leave you alone. I only thought of eating you because I was so hungry.”

Inside, the man brought out the endless bread, meat, and wine. The giant ate and drank heartily and was very pleased. Afterward, the man asked if the giant could show him the castle of Stromberg. The giant said he’d check his map, which showed all towns and villages. But he couldn’t find the castle. “No problem,” he said. “I have larger maps upstairs.” They searched those but still found no sign of it.

The man wanted to continue his journey, but the giant asked him to stay a day or two until his brother returned from gathering food. When the brother came back, they asked him about the castle. He said he’d look after eating. Once fed, they all went to his room to search through more maps. After some time, they found the castle, but it was thousands of miles away. “How will I reach it?” the man asked.

“I’ve two hours free,” the giant offered. “I’ll carry you close to the castle, then I must return to care for the child we’re watching.” The giant carried the man to within about a hundred leagues of Stromberg castle, then left him, saying, “You can walk the rest yourself.” The man traveled day and night until he reached the golden castle, which sat atop a glass mountain. Looking up, he saw the cursed maiden driving around her castle before going inside. He was overjoyed but couldn’t climb the slippery mountain; every attempt made him fall back. Saddened, he decided to wait for her and built a small hut to live in, watching her daily for a whole year.

One day, from his hut, he saw three robbers fighting and called out, “God be with you.” They stopped temporarily, looked around, then returned to fighting more fiercely. He called again twice more, and the third time, curious, he went out and asked why they were fighting. One said he had found a stick that could open any door by striking it. Another had a cloak making its wearer invisible. The third caught a horse that could overcome any obstacle, even climb glass mountains. They couldn’t agree whether to keep these treasures together or split up. The man said, “I’ll trade you something far more valuable—not money, since I have none, but something better. First, I’ll test if your things really work.” The robbers gave him the horse, stick, and cloak. Putting on the cloak, he vanished from sight. Then he struck at them with the stick, beating them one by one, shouting, “There, you lazy scoundrels, now are you satisfied?”

He mounted the horse and rode up the glass mountain. At the castle gate, he found it closed but struck it with the stick, and the door flew open. He climbed the steps and entered the room where the maiden sat with a golden goblet of wine. Still invisible under the cloak, he threw the ring she had given him into her cup. “That’s my own ring!” she exclaimed. “If that’s true, then the man destined to free me must be here.”

She searched everywhere but couldn’t find him. Meanwhile, he had gone outside, mounted the horse, and removed his cloak. When she reached the castle gate, she saw him and cried for joy. He got off the horse, took her in his arms, and she kissed him. “Now you have truly freed me,” she said. “Tomorrow we will celebrate our wedding.”
