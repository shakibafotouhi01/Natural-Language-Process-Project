HANSEL AND GRETEL

Near a vast forest lived a poor woodcutter with his wife and their two children, a boy named Hansel and a girl named Gretel. They had barely enough to eat, and when a great famine came over the land, the man could no longer find enough bread for their daily meal. One night, as he lay worried in bed, he sighed and said to his wife, "What will become of us? How can we feed the children when there is nothing left even for ourselves?" 

The woman replied, "Here’s what we’ll do: early tomorrow morning, we’ll take the children deep into the forest. We’ll light a fire, give each a last piece of bread, then leave and go back to work. They won’t find their way home, and we’ll be rid of them." 

"No," the man protested, "I cannot abandon my children to the forest—they will be torn to pieces by wild animals." 

"Oh, you fool!" the woman snapped. "If we starve together, what’s the difference? We might as well build our own coffins." She nagged him until he agreed, though he felt sorry for the children.

Hansel and Gretel, too hungry to sleep, overheard their stepmother’s plan. Gretel cried, "It’s all over for us." 

"Don't worry," Hansel said. "I’ll find a way to help us." When their parents were asleep, he quietly got out of bed, put on his little coat, and slipped outside. The bright moonlight made the white pebbles in front of the house shine like silver coins. Hansel gathered as many as he could into his pocket and went back inside. "Sleep well, Gretel," he said. "God won’t abandon us."

At dawn, before the sun rose, the woman called the children from bed. "Get up, lazybones! We’re going to the forest to gather wood." She handed each a small piece of bread and warned them not to eat it all before the midday meal. Gretel hid her bread under her apron, and Hansel kept his pebbles safe in his pocket. The four set off together.

After walking a short distance, Hansel kept stopping and looking back at the house. His father asked, "Why do you lag behind?" 

"I’m watching my little white cat sitting on the roof, saying goodbye," Hansel lied.

"Fool," his stepmother said. "That’s not a cat; it’s the morning sun shining on the chimney." But Hansel was secretly dropping pebbles along the path.

When they reached the heart of the forest, the father told the children to gather wood while he lit a fire. Hansel and Gretel piled brush high like a small hill. When the fire blazed, the woman told them, "Rest by the fire, we’ll go fetch more wood and come back for you."

The children sat by the flames and at noon ate a small piece of bread. They heard what sounded like the ax chopping wood and thought their father was close. But it was only a branch tied to a dead tree swaying in the wind. The children, tired, fell asleep as the sun set.

At night, Gretel woke crying, "How will we get out now?" 

"Wait for the moon," Hansel said. When it rose full and bright, he took Gretel’s hand and followed the shining pebbles to find their way home. 

After walking all night, they reached their house at dawn. The woman was angry, scolding them for sleeping so long in the forest. Their father was happy, for leaving them had broken his heart.

Soon another famine struck, and again the children heard their stepmother saying, "We have but half a loaf left; the children must go deeper into the forest where they can’t return." The man’s heart ached, but the woman was relentless, and he gave in once more.

This time, Hansel tried to gather pebbles again, but the door was locked. He comforted Gretel, "Don’t cry, God will help us."

The next morning, the woman gave them even smaller pieces of bread. Hansel, unable to collect pebbles, crumbled his bread and dropped crumbs along the way. Again, he paused often to look back.

"I’m watching my little pigeon goodbye on the roof," he said.

"Fool!" said his stepmother. "That’s the morning sun, not a pigeon."

The woman led them deeper into the forest, to a place they had never been. She lit a fire and told them to sit and rest while she went for wood. At noon, Gretel shared her tiny bread with Hansel, who had nothing left. They fell asleep, but no one returned. After dark, Hansel told Gretel to wait for the moon so they could follow the crumbs. But when they tried, the crumbs were gone—birds had eaten them all.

"We’ll find our way," Hansel said bravely, but they wandered all night and all the next day, growing weak from hunger. They found only a few berries, lay down under a tree, and fell asleep.

Three days after leaving home, they set off again, but only went deeper into the forest. Near midday, they saw a beautiful white bird singing on a bough. After it finished, it flew ahead, leading them to a small house with a roof of bread, walls of cake, and windows of clear sugar.

"We’ll eat this!" Hansel said eagerly, breaking off a piece of roof. Gretel nibbled a sugar window pane. Suddenly, a voice called from inside:

"Nibble, nibble, gnaw, who’s nibbling at my little house?"

"The wind, the wind, the heaven-born wind," the children replied, ignoring the warning and eating more.

Then the door swung open, and an old woman with crutches came out. Hansel and Gretel dropped their treats, frightened. She smiled kindly and said, "Dear children, who brought you here? Come in and stay with me. No harm will come to you." She held their hands and led them inside, where she served them milk, pancakes, sugar, apples, and nuts. She made two cozy beds for them, and the children felt they were in heaven.

But the woman was actually a wicked witch who trapped children to eat them. She had built the house to lure them in. Witches have red eyes and sharp noses, sensing when humans are near. When Hansel and Gretel came close, she laughed wickedly, "I have them now; they shall not escape!"

Early the next morning, before the children woke, she locked Hansel in a tiny stable with a grated door. He screamed and struggled, but it was no use. She woke Gretel, shaking her, and ordered, "Get up, lazy girl! Fetch water and cook for your brother. He needs to be fattened up for my cooking." Gretel sobbed but had to obey.

Hansel was fed the best food, while Gretel got only crab shells. Every morning the witch stuck her finger through the bars to feel if Hansel was fat yet. Hansel cleverly held out a thin bone instead, fooling her poor eyesight. Weeks passed, and Hansel remained thin. The witch grew impatient and threatened, "Fat or thin, tomorrow I’ll kill and cook him." Gretel cried, begging God for help, wishing they’d been eaten by forest beasts so they could have died together.

One morning, the witch made Gretel stoke the oven’s fire and ordered her to check if it was hot enough to bake bread. She pushed Gretel toward the blazing oven, planning to shut her inside and cook her too. But Gretel pretended not to understand. "How do I get in?" she asked.

"The door is big enough," the witch said, crawling in herself to show her. Quick as a flash, Gretel shoved the witch deep into the oven, closed the iron door, and locked it tight. The witch screamed horribly as she burned.

Gretel ran to the stable and freed Hansel. "We’re saved! The witch is dead!" she cried. Hansel flew out like a bird from a cage. They hugged, danced, and kissed with joy.

Freed, they explored the witch’s house and found chests filled with pearls and jewels. Hansel stuffed his pockets with treasures, and Gretel filled her apron. "Now we must leave," Hansel said, "and get out of the witch’s forest."

After two hours, they came to a wide river. "We can’t cross—no plank or bridge," Hansel said.

"But look! That white duck is swimming. I’ll ask her to carry us across," Gretel offered. She called:

"Little duck, little duck, can you see
Hansel and Gretel waiting for thee?
No plank or bridge is anywhere near,
Take us across on your back so dear."

The duck came over. Hansel sat on her back, then Gretel, but she was too heavy for both at once. So the duck carried them one at a time safely across.

Once on the other side, the forest grew familiar, and soon they saw their father’s house. They ran inside and threw themselves into his arms. Their father had never known a happy moment since abandoning them; their stepmother, however, was dead.

Gretel emptied her apron, spilling pearls and jewels across the floor, and Hansel emptied his pockets. Their troubles were over, and they lived happily together forever.

The story ends here—there runs a mouse; whoever catches it, may make a big fur cap from it.
