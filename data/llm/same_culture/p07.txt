The smart rabbit

Far away from here, there was once a beautiful valley covered in trees, surrounded by tall mountains. A powerful river flowed through this valley, watering all the different trees and plants that grew there. Many animals lived in this peaceful valley—rabbits, birds, squirrels, and deer. They all lived happily because no wolves or lions lived there to hunt them.

One day, however, a wolf climbed down from the mountains and entered the valley. As soon as he arrived, he began chasing the helpless animals, eating them one by one. Only rarely did any animal manage to run away unharmed, but all the others feared it would soon be their turn.

Worried, the animals turned to the wise old owl and asked him to find a way to get rid of the wolf. The owl said there was no way to fight the wolf; his sharp teeth and powerful paws were stronger than any other animal in the valley. The owl advised them to learn to live with the wolf.

The animals protested they couldn’t live in constant fear, so they made a desperate plan: each day, one animal would be chosen to go to the wolf and be eaten. That way, the rest could live in peace, knowing the wolf had already had his meal for the day.

The wolf, tired of the chase and pleased with the idea of food coming to him, agreed to this plan without hesitation.

The next morning, the animals gathered and decided that the little rabbit—the smallest and weakest in the valley—would be the one to be given to the wolf.

Terrified, the rabbit first tried to run away but soon realized there was nowhere to hide. He thought about fighting but knew the wolf was far too strong. So he quietly walked to the wolf’s den and called out, “Oh wolf! Oh wolf! Come out of your lair, for I am your supper today.”

The wolf came out and sniffed the rabbit hungrily. “What a delicious little meal you will make!” said the wolf. “I can’t believe my luck to find a valley where animals bring themselves to me.”

“It’s true,” the rabbit sighed. “I came here on my own four feet because I know I can’t escape my fate. Besides, a mighty wolf like you—though not the scariest or strongest in this valley—is still hard to escape.”

The vain wolf was stunned. “What? I’m not the scariest or strongest? I’m the only wolf here! There’s no other wolf in this valley!” he cried angrily.

“You don’t know about the other wolf,” said the rabbit. “Anyway, you should eat me now, because even if I escape you, no animal could escape the other, scarier wolf.” Then the rabbit tried to climb into the wolf’s mouth.

The wolf was offended and shook the rabbit out. “Take me to this other wolf,” he said, “and I will spare you for today, tasty morsel. Show me where this wolf lives who thinks he’s better than me.”

The rabbit sighed and said, “What does it matter? In the end, I will be eaten by a wolf—whether it’s you or the other with bigger teeth and stronger legs. Follow me.”

“Hmph!” said the wolf. “We’ll see who’s stronger. Lead the way!”

The wolf followed the rabbit until they reached an old, abandoned well.

“There,” said the rabbit, “is the lair of the other wolf, who is stronger and fiercer than you. Just look down into the well and you’ll see him resting after his last meal.”

The wolf jumped up on the edge of the well and peered down into the darkness.

“I don’t see anything; it’s too dark!” he said.

“You have to look closer,” the rabbit said. “Put your whole head down into the well and you’ll see him looking back.”

So the wolf bent over and stuck his head into the well. After a moment, his eyes adjusted and he saw his own reflection in the water below, as if another wolf was staring back.

“Aha! Now I see you, you coward!” the wolf shouted into the well. His voice echoed back to him.

“Did you just call me a coward? How dare you! Come out, and we’ll see who’s the fiercer wolf!” the wolf yelled, but again the echo returned.

The rabbit watched the wolf argue with himself and said, “I don’t think he’s coming out. Of course, the bigger, scarier wolf will chase the smaller, less scary one.”

The wolf, believing this, jumped into the well to catch his “enemy.” But because he could not swim, he never came out, and the valley was freed from the evil wolf—all thanks to a small, clever rabbit.
