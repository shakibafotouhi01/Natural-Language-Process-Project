THE FISHERMAN AND HIS WIFE

Once there was a fisherman who lived with his wife in a filthy pigsty by the seaside. Every day, he went out to fish. One day, as he sat on the shore with his fishing rod, watching the sparkling waves, suddenly his float was pulled underwater. When he reeled it in, he caught a huge fish. But to his surprise, the fish spoke, “Please, let me live! I’m not just a fish—I’m an enchanted prince. Put me back in the water and let me go!” The fisherman said, “Well, I don’t want anything to do with a talking fish, so you might as well swim away!” He put the fish back, and it swam straight down, leaving a long trail of blood behind.

When he returned to the pigsty, he told his wife about the big fish that spoke and how he let it go. His wife said, “Didn’t you ask it for anything? We live so badly in this dirty pigsty. Go back and ask the fish for a cozy little cottage.” The fisherman was reluctant but went back to the shore. The water looked yellow and green as he called out:

“O man of the sea!  
Listen to me!  
My wife Ilsabill  
Insists on her will,  
And sends me to beg a favor from thee!”

The fish appeared and asked what his wife wanted. The fisherman said she wished for a little cottage instead of the pigsty. “Go home,” the fish said, “she is already in the cottage.” He went back and saw his wife standing in front of a neat little house, with a parlor, bedroom, kitchen, and behind it a garden full of flowers and fruits, and a courtyard with ducks and chickens. “How happily we’ll live now!” the fisherman said. “At least we’ll try,” she replied.

After a while, Ilsabill said, “This cottage is too small. The yard and garden don’t have enough space. I want a large stone castle.” The fisherman was uneasy but went to the shore again. The sea looked blue and gloomy, but calm, as he repeated his plea to the man of the sea.

The fish asked what she wanted this time. “A stone castle,” the fisherman said sadly. “Go home,” the fish said, “She’s already at the castle gate.”

The fisherman found his wife standing before a grand castle, with many servants, richly furnished rooms with golden chairs and tables, a garden, and a large park full of sheep, goats, hares, and deer. There were stables and cowhouses in the courtyard. “Now we can live happily here,” said the fisherman. “Maybe,” his wife said, “but let’s sleep on it.”

The next morning, Ilsabill woke him up, saying, “Get up, husband, we should be king of all the land.” “Why be king?” he asked. “I won’t.” “Then I will,” she said. “But how? The fish can’t make you king.” “Say no more,” she said. “Go ask!”

The man was sad but went. The sea was dark grey, with curling waves and foamy ridges as he called out:

“O man of the sea!  
Listen to me!  
My wife Ilsabill  
Insists on her will,  
And sends me to beg a favor from thee!”

“What does she want now?” asked the fish. “She wants to be king.” “Go home, she is king already.”

At the palace, soldiers marched with drums and trumpets. Inside, Ilsabill sat on a throne of gold and diamonds, wearing a golden crown. Six tall maidens stood beside her. “Are you king?” the fisherman asked. “Yes,” she said. “What a fine thing to be king! Now we’ll never want anything else.” “Never is a long time,” she said. “I’m king but tired of it. I want to be emperor.” “Why want to be emperor?” he asked. “Go to the fish! I insist.” “I’m sure the fish can’t make you emperor.” “I’m king, and you’re my servant, so go!”

He went, worried the fish might be tired of granting wishes. The sea was black and muddy, waves whipped by a great whirlwind. Still, he called out to the sea:

“O man of the sea!  
Listen to me!  
My wife Ilsabill  
Insists on her will,  
And sends me to beg a favor from thee!”

“Emperor?” asked the fish. “Yes.” “Go home, she’s emperor already.”

Back home, she sat on a lofty golden throne, wearing a crown two yards high. Guards stood by, from giants to dwarfs. Princes, dukes, and earls surrounded her. “Are you emperor?” he asked. “Yes.” “What a thing to be emperor!” “But I want to be pope next.” “How can you be pope? There’s only one pope.” “I’ll be pope today.” “The fish can’t make you pope.” “If he made me emperor, he can make me pope. Go!”

The fisherman went again. The sea was wild, waves boiling, ships tossed, the sky partly red as a storm brewed. Terrified, he called:

“O man of the sea!  
Listen to me!  
My wife Ilsabill  
Insists on her will,  
And sends me to beg a favor from thee!”

“Pope?” asked the fish. “Yes.” “Go home, she’s pope already.”

He found her on a throne two miles high, wearing three great crowns. Around her was all the Church’s power, with rows of huge to tiny burning lights beside her. “Wife, is this pope?” “Yes.” “It’s grand; now you must be satisfied.” “I’ll think about it,” she said. That night she couldn’t sleep, planning what next to be. At sunrise, she looked out and said angrily, “I can’t stop the sun from rising. Husband, go to the fish and tell him I want to rule the sun and moon.”

Half-asleep, he was so frightened he fell out of bed. “Can’t you be satisfied with being pope?” “No, I won’t be, if the sun and moon keep rising without asking me. Go now!”

He went trembling. A fierce storm came up, shaking trees and rocks, with black clouds, lightning, thunder, and huge waves. The fisherman stumbled to the shore and called:

“O man of the sea!  
Listen to me!  
My wife Ilsabill  
Insists on her will,  
And sends me to beg a favor from thee!”

“Ruler of sun and moon?” the fish said. “Go home... to your pigsty again.”

So back they went, living in the pigsty to this day.
