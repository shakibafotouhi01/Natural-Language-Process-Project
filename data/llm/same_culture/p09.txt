4 - Zal and Rodabeh

Soon Zal desired to explore the kingdom. He set out with a grand escort and, after some travel, proudly entered Kabul. Mihrab, the ruler there and a descendant of the serpent Zohak, was wise and noble. Hearing that Zal, son of Sam and to whom he paid tribute, was approaching, Mihrab went out to greet him with his nobles and slaves bearing valuable gifts. Zal, upon learning of Mihrab’s arrival, prepared a feast in his tents. Mihrab and his retinue dined with him late into the night. After the guests left, Zal praised Mihrab’s beauty. Then a nobleman stood and told him, “O Zal, you know not true beauty since you have not seen this man’s daughter. She is like a slender cypress, her face shines brighter than the sun, and her mouth is like a pomegranate blossom.”

Hearing this, Zal’s heart was filled with longing, and he could not sleep, thinking of her beauty. At dawn, he opened his court doors, and the nobles gathered according to rank. Mihrab arrived to greet him from outside the city gates. Zal wanted Mihrab to ask a favor of him. Mihrab spoke: “O mighty ruler, I ask only that you stay as a guest under my roof so my heart may rejoice in your presence.” Zal answered, “O King, do not ask this favor; it cannot be done. The Shah and Sam would be angry if they knew I dined under the roof of Zohak’s kin. Ask for anything else.”

Mihrab left sadly, bowing before Zal. Zal watched him go and once more praised him. Then his thoughts returned to the King’s daughter and her beauty; days passed unnoticed as desire consumed him.

One morning Mihrab visited the women’s quarters, where he found Sindokht, his wife, and their daughter Rodabeh. The house was like a garden full of color and sweet scents, and the two women shone like moons of beauty. After greeting Rodabeh, Mihrab marveled at her loveliness and blessed her. Sindokht asked Mihrab about the stranger in their city’s tents: “Tell me, what kind of man is this white-haired son of Sam? Is he worthy of our nest, or even the throne?”

Mihrab replied, “O fair cypress, the son of Sam is a hero among men. His heart is like a lion’s, his strength an elephant’s. He is graceful to friends, a fierce crocodile to foes. Even his white locks add to his glory, turning flaws into beauty.” Hearing this, Rodabeh’s heart burned with love for Zal. She could neither eat nor rest; she was like one transformed. Unable to bear it alone, she confided in her beloved slaves, begging them to keep her secret and help ease her troubled heart.

Fearful, the slaves begged her to let go of a man marked by men and rejected by his own father, but Rodabeh would not listen. Seeing her resolve, the wisest among them spoke: “O moon-faced beauty and slender cypress, it shall be done as you wish. We will not rest until this royal youth lies at your feet.”

Rodabeh was glad and promised to plant a noble tree for the faithful slave, which would bear riches, jewels, and wisdom. The slaves went to work, knowing their plan must be subtle. Dressed in fine clothes, they cheerfully entered the garden by the river outside the city, picking roses and scattering blossoms in the water to tell fortunes. There they came near Zal’s tents. Zal noticed them and asked who they were; one said, “They are slaves sent by the moon of Kabul into the garden of flowers.”

Zal’s heart leapt with joy. He went to the river with one page. Seeing a waterbird flying up, he shot and struck it through the heart, and it fell near the rose-gatherers. He sent the boy to retrieve it. The moon-faced women pressed around the lad, asking, “Who is this archer? Surely he is king among men.” The boy answered, “Don’t you know the son of Sam, the hero? There is no equal in strength or beauty.”

The women objected, “Nay, boast not so. The house of Mihrab holds a sun that outshines all else.” The boy smiled and returned to Zal. Zal asked why he smiled so, and he told him what the women said. Zal sent him back with gifts of pearl and gold, asking them to stay and carry jewels with their roses.

The slave who had sworn to serve Rodabeh asked to see Zal’s face, saying, “A secret known by three is no secret.” Zal allowed it, and she told him of Rodabeh’s beauty. His passion grew, and he asked her to show the way to see the maiden. The slave said, “Let us return to the women’s house. We will praise the son of Sam to Rodabeh and weave the net that will trap the lion in his chase of the lamb.”

Zal agreed, and the women returned, joyful, saying, “The lion is entering the snare, and the wishes of Rodabeh and Zal will come to pass.” Yet, at the city gates, a porter scolded them for leaving while the stranger stayed in Kabul. They calmed his anger and reached Rodabeh.

They told her of Zal’s beauty and prowess. She smiled and said, “Why have you changed your tune? You once mocked this bird-reared youth with the sage’s locks, but now sing his praises with loud voices.”

Rodabeh secretly prepared her home to honor a guest, adorning it with brocades from Rome and silks from India, perfumed with musk and ambergris, and blossoming with flowers. As night fell, a slave came to Zal, whispering, “Come, all is ready.” He followed her.

At the women’s house, Zal saw Rodabeh standing on the roof. Her beauty was like a cypress under a full moon. She welcomed him: “O young man, son of a hero, Heaven’s blessings upon you!” Zal replied with prayers to meet her more closely, for she was above on the roof, and he below.

Rodabeh let down her long hair, saying, “Here is a flawless rope. Climb, O warrior, and seize my dark locks; it is fitting I ensnare you.” Zal replied, “No, fair one; I would ill become you if I harmed you.” He kissed her hair, called for a rope, tied a running knot, threw it up, and climbed to the roof.

Hand in hand, they entered the golden chambers, surrounded by slaves. They gazed upon one another, recognizing great beauty, and hours passed with sweet conversation as love grew. Zal said, “O cypress, scented with musk, when Minuchihr and Sam learn this, they will be angry, accusing me of forgetting God and lifting their hands against me. But I swear this life is worthless if not spent with you. I call upon Heaven to hear me: none but you shall be my bride.”

Rodabeh swore the same oath. Dawn’s drums sounded from the King’s tents, announcing the day’s arrival. Zal and Rodabeh cried together, “O glory of the world, stay a while, do not come so quickly!” But the sun paid no heed, and it was time to part. Zal let himself down from the battlements and left his beloved’s house.

When daylight filled the earth, Zal received the usual greetings from nobles and chiefs. He gathered his Mubids (wise men) and told them of his love for a daughter of the serpent lineage. The Mubids were troubled and kept silent; none wished to poison the sweetness of this love. Zal urged them to speak, promising rich gifts. They said a king’s honor is not hurt by a woman, and though Mihrab was descended from Zohak, he was noble and brave. They advised Zal to write to his father and ask Sam to appear before the Shah.

Zal called a scribe and dictated his letter. He told Sam of his love and fears, reminded him of being cast out at birth, raised by a bird in a nest beneath the sun, while his father lived in silk. He did not defend what happened, only recalled Sam’s promise. He gave the letter to a messenger to ride swiftly to Sam.

Sam was troubled when he heard. “Woe is me. The wild bird’s fosterling seeks to unite with a cursed race.” He pondered his reply: if he forbade Zal’s desire, he’d break his oath; if he allowed it, what could come from the union of a Deev and a bird’s son? Burdened, he called his Mubids to read the stars, for blending fire and water could bring disaster.

All day they cast the horoscope for Zal and Rodabeh. At dusk, they returned rejoicing. To Sam they said, “Hail, Sam, for we have read the skies; A clear spring will bring a son to Zal, a hero unmatched in power and glory in Iran.”

Sam’s heart lifted. He rewarded the wise men and sent the messenger back to Zal with words: “I find your passion folly, son, but bound by oath, it shall be fulfilled. I go to Iran to plead your case to the Shah.” Sam assembled his army and set out to Iran with trumpets and cymbals heralding his arrival.

When the messenger returned to Zal, he praised God and gave gifts to the poor and his servants. Yet no sleep touched Zal’s eyes, nor could he find rest; his soul burned with longing. One day, a slave came with Sam’s letter for Rodabeh. She rejoiced and sent back a precious crown and ring.

On leaving her chamber, she met Queen Sindokht, who questioned her sharply about her comings and goings. The slave, trembling, lied that she sold jewels and robes. Sindokht demanded proof and found the tiara Rodabeh had embroidered in the slave’s sleeve. Angered, Sindokht ordered the slave chained and called her daughter.

To Rodabeh she said, “O moon of noble race, blind to all but goodness, how have you strayed on evil paths? Confide in me. From whom come your gifts? For which man are they meant?” Rodabeh, ashamed, confessed all.

Sindokht feared the Shah’s wrath and wept alone in sorrow. Mihrab entered and found her grieving. She told him of Rodabeh’s love for Zal. Mihrab’s heart troubled as well, knowing Kabul could not withstand the Shah’s might.

Minuchihr, too, was disturbed, fearing this union was Ahriman’s evil plot. He ordered Nauder to summon Sam. Sam came gladly before the Shah, kissing the ground and blessing him. Minuchihr welcomed him, asked of the wars and the Mazinderan Deevs, and Sam told his battles. The Shah rejoiced at his valor, praised him, and called for a feast where heroes drank and feasted through the night.

At dawn, the king’s court opened for petitioners. Sam came first to speak of Zal’s love, but Minuchihr would not let him speak fully. Instead, he commanded: “Go with your army. March on Kabul, burn Mihrab’s house, destroy the serpent’s seed; let none survive. The earth must be rid of this brood.”

Sam saw the Shah’s anger and knew words would not help. He bowed on the earth and said, “I obey.” His army advanced, shaking the land. News of Sam’s approach brought despair to Kabul. Mihrab’s house was filled with weeping. But Zal was angry and went to his father’s camp, asking for an audience. Sam granted it.

Zal reminded Sam of his oath, begging mercy for Kabul’s innocents. Sam’s heart moved. “You speak rightly, my son. I have been unjust to you since birth. Hold your wrath; I will find a remedy. You shall bear a letter to the Shah, and when he sees you, his heart will soften.”

Zal kissed his father’s hand and received blessings. Sam wrote the letter recounting his deeds for Iran, his wars, his slaying of the dragon, and the peace he brought. Growing old, he could no longer fight, but his brave son followed him. Yet his son’s heart was consumed by love—a longing that might kill him if denied. Sam entrusted the matter to the Shah’s wisdom.

Zal set out with the letter, accompanied by the kingdom’s finest. Fear gripped Kabul; Mihrab wondered how to calm the King. He said to Sindokht, “The King is enraged because of you and Rodabeh. Since I cannot face him, I will lead Rodabeh to his court and kill her before his eyes. Perhaps his wrath will ease thus.”

Sindokht, quick-witted, kept silent, then asked for the treasury key. “Let me take what I need and go to Sam. Perhaps I can sway him to spare Kabul.” Mihrab, distraught, agreed.

Sindokht left, bringing with her immense gifts: three hundred thousand gold coins, sixty silver-trimmed horses with slaves bearing cups of musk and precious stones, two hundred camels, and four elephants from India laden with brocade and carpets. Her retinue stretched two miles beyond Kabul’s gates.

In Seistan, Sindokht asked Sam’s guards for entrance. Sam welcomed her, and she bowed, blessing him. She laid out the treasures, marveling at their worth. Sam wondered why a woman bore gifts of such value from a land so rich. Accepting the gifts, he said, “Let these be given to my son’s treasurer.”

Sindokht, encouraged, asked Sam why he wished to destroy Kabul. Sam demanded, “Are you Mihrab’s slave or wife? Is she really your daughter whom Zal saw? Tell me of her beauty that I may judge if she is worthy of my son.”

Sindokht replied, “Swear to me first that you will spare my life and those dear to me.” Sam took her hand and swore a solemn oath.

Freed of fear, Sindokht revealed all: her lineage from Zohak, her marriage to Mihrab, and her daughter Rodabeh’s love for Zal. She asked Sam to punish only the wicked in Kabul and spare the innocent, lest day turn to night.

Sam vowed, “By my life and oath, I shall protect you and your people. Zal shall marry Rodabeh, though she be from a foreign race.” He told Sindokht about his letter to the Shah pleading Zal’s case and that Zal was away delivering it. “Tell me of Rodabeh,” he pleaded.

Sindokht said, “If Zal wishes to gladden our hearts, he must come himself and see our moon with his own eyes.”

Sam smiled and replied, “Rest easy; all will end as you desire.” He loaded her with gifts and sent her away. Sindokht’s face shone with hope.

Meanwhile, Zal arrived at Minuchihr’s court. He knelt and kissed the ground before the Shah. Moved, Minuchihr ordered musk sprinkled before him and raised him to his side. Zal gave the Shah Sam’s letter. Reading it, the king was pained but promised to fulfill Zal’s wish. He asked him to remain a while for counsel.

The nobles and Shah feasted all night. At dawn, Zal presented himself for audience. The Shah’s wise men studied the stars for three days, then declared joyfully: “The son of Sam and daughter of Mihrab shall be a glorious pair. Their son will be a mighty warrior who will expand Iran’s glory, defeat evildoers, and bring prosperity.”

Minuchihr commanded secrecy. He tested Zal’s wisdom with riddles asked by the wise men. Zal answered each with insight, impressing all.

The Shah then tested Zal’s strength. Watching from his palace roof, he saw Zal outperform all in archery, spear-throwing, and wrestling. The nobles cheered; Minuchihr showered Zal with gifts.

The Shah replied to Sam’s letter: “O brave hero, I have seen your son and find him worthy. I grant your wish and send him back to you satisfied. May no enemy harm him.”

After receiving the king’s leave, Zal returned proudly. Sam rejoiced upon receiving the letter. The army prepared to march; a messenger was sent to Mihrab telling him that Sam and Zal were coming to Kabul.

At the news, Mihrab relaxed and ordered his army to dress festively. Silk banners and music filled the city. Sindokht shared the good news with Rodabeh, and they adorned the house as a paradise with golden carpets, ivory thrones, and scented floors.

As the guests neared Kabul, Mihrab went out to meet them and crowned Zal with diamonds. They entered the city triumphantly. The people bowed before them. Sindokht met them at the palace, scattering musk and jewels.

Sam, receiving their homage, smiled and asked Sindokht, “How much longer will you hide Rodabeh from us?” Sindokht replied, “What will you give to see the sun?”

Sam answered, “Anything you wish—my slaves and throne as well.”

Sindokht led him inside. Sam beheld Rodabeh and was struck speechless by her beauty, beyond all dreams. He asked Mihrab for her hand, and the marriage was arranged according to custom.

The lovers sat upon a throne as Mihrab read an endless list of gifts. They feasted joyfully for seven days. After a month, Sam returned to Seistan with Zal and Rodabeh following. Soon Sam went again to war, leaving the kingdom to his son. Zal ruled wisely beside Rodabeh, who wore a golden crown and sat as queen at his side.
